# gibbor (גִּבּוֹר)

**Strong's:** H1368
**Root:** ג-ב-ר (*g-b-r*) — "to be strong, to prevail, to be mighty"
**Part of speech:** Adjective (used substantivally as a noun); sometimes classified as a noun outright
**Glosses:** mighty man, warrior, hero, champion, mighty one

## Root family (g-b-r)

The root ג-ב-ר generates a tight cluster of related words:

- **גָּבַר** (*gavar*, H1396) — verb, "to be strong, to prevail, to be mighty." Qal: to be strong; Piel: to make strong; Hithpael: to show oneself mighty. ~25 occurrences.
- **גֶּבֶר** (*gever*, H1397) — noun, "man" (with emphasis on strength/virility, as distinct from אִישׁ *ish* which is more generic). ~66 occurrences.
- **גְּבוּרָה** (*gevurah*, H1369) — noun, "strength, might, power, mighty deed." ~61 occurrences. This is the abstract noun of the root — the quality that a *gibbor* possesses.
- **גַּבְרִיאֵל** (*Gavriel*, H1403) — proper name, "Gabriel," meaning "mighty one of God" or "God is my strength." The angel's name is built from this root + אֵל (*El*, God).

The root carries the core idea of **prevailing strength** — not merely physical size but the capacity to overcome, to dominate in contest, to prove superior. This is why it can describe warriors, but also God himself.

## Semantic range

גִּבּוֹר covers a broader range than any single English word captures:

1. **Warrior / fighting man.** The most common use. A *gibbor* is a man who fights and prevails. This is the default sense in military contexts — the professional soldier, the combat-proven champion. Often in the plural גִּבּוֹרִים (*gibborim*).

2. **Hero / champion.** Beyond mere soldiering — a man of exceptional prowess who stands out from the ranks. David's "mighty men" (2 Samuel 23) are called *gibborim* not because they served in the army but because they performed extraordinary feats.

3. **Mighty one / powerful person.** Extended beyond combat to anyone who exercises dominant strength or influence. Nimrod is called a *gibbor* in Genesis 10:8-9 — his might is expressed in hunting and kingdom-building, not (explicitly) in battle.

4. **Tyrant / violent one.** In some contexts the word carries a negative edge — the *gibborim* of Genesis 6:4 ("mighty men of old, men of renown") are associated with the corruption that precipitated the flood. The word itself is neutral, but context can shade it toward arrogance or oppression.

5. **Divine title.** When applied to God, *gibbor* ascends to its highest register. אֵל גִּבּוֹר (*El Gibbor*) — "Mighty God" — appears as a messianic title in Isaiah 9:6 and as a divine epithet in Isaiah 10:21, Deuteronomy 10:17, Jeremiah 32:18, and Nehemiah 9:32. Here the word means not just "strong" but "omnipotently prevailing."

**Distribution:** גִּבּוֹר occurs approximately 159 times in the OT. The heaviest concentrations are in the historical books (Judges, Samuel, Kings, Chronicles) where military narratives dominate, and in the prophets where God's might is contrasted with human pretension.

**Key distinction from synonyms:**
- אִישׁ (*ish*) = a man (generic, social identity)
- גֶּבֶר (*gever*) = a man (emphasis on strength/virility)
- גִּבּוֹר (*gibbor*) = a mighty man (emphasis on proven, prevailing power)

The progression is real but should not be pressed too hard — context governs, not etymology.

## Key occurrences

- **Genesis 6:4** — The נְפִלִים (*Nephilim*) are described alongside the גִּבּוֹרִים, "mighty men of old, men of renown" (אַנְשֵׁי הַשֵּׁם). The term here carries ambiguity: these *gibborim* may be heroic or violent or both. The narrative context (escalating wickedness, 6:5) tilts the connotation toward arrogant, domineering power.

- **Genesis 10:8-9** — Nimrod: "He was the first גִּבּוֹר on earth. He was a mighty hunter before the LORD" (גִּבֹּר צַיִד לִפְנֵי יהוה). Nimrod's *gibbor* status is expressed through hunting and empire-building (Babel, Nineveh). Whether "before the LORD" is favorable or defiant is debated; BDB notes both possibilities.

- **Judges 6:12** — The angel addresses Gideon: "The LORD is with you, גִּבּוֹר הֶחָיִל" — "mighty man of valor." This is the phrase under study. Gideon is threshing wheat in a winepress out of fear of the Midianites — the title is either prophetic (declaring what God will make him) or ironic (exposing the gap between calling and current reality). See the combined-phrase analysis below and the *chayil* entry.

- **Joshua 1:14** — Moses' command to the Transjordan tribes: "All the גִּבּוֹרֵי הַחַיִל (*gibborey hachayil*) shall cross over armed before your brothers." Here the phrase is straightforwardly military: the combat-ready warriors.

- **2 Samuel 23:8-39** — David's *gibborim*: the list of his "mighty men," the elite warriors who performed extraordinary feats. The section opens: "These are the names of the גִּבּוֹרִים whom David had." This is the archetype of the *gibbor* as champion — men who broke through enemy lines, stood alone against hundreds, fought until their hands froze to the sword.

- **1 Chronicles 12:1-22** — Warriors who came to David at Ziklag and Hebron, repeatedly described as גִּבּוֹרֵי חַיִל (*gibborey chayil*), "mighty men of valor." Verse 21 uses the phrase of Manassite defectors; verse 8 describes Gadite warriors whose "faces were like the faces of lions."

- **Isaiah 9:6 (Hebrew 9:5)** — The messianic child is given the name אֵל גִּבּוֹר (*El Gibbor*), "Mighty God." This is the most theologically loaded use of *gibbor* in the OT. The word that elsewhere describes human warriors is here fused with אֵל to form a divine title applied to the coming king. The LXX translators struggled with this — some manuscripts render it variously — but the Hebrew is unambiguous: this child bears the name "Mighty God."

- **Isaiah 10:21** — "A remnant shall return... to אֵל גִּבּוֹר, the Mighty God." The same title from 9:6 reappears, now clearly applied to YHWH himself. This back-reference confirms that the title in 9:6 is genuinely divine, not merely honorific.

- **Deuteronomy 10:17** — "For the LORD your God is God of gods and Lord of lords, הָאֵל הַגָּדֹל הַגִּבֹּר וְהַנּוֹרָא — the great God, the mighty and the awesome." Here *gibbor* stands in a triad of divine attributes: great, mighty, fearsome.

- **Jeremiah 32:18** — Jeremiah's prayer: "the great, the mighty God (הָאֵל הַגָּדוֹל הַגִּבּוֹר), the LORD of hosts is his name." Same triad as Deuteronomy 10:17, now in prophetic prayer.

- **Psalm 24:8** — "Who is this King of glory? The LORD, strong and mighty (עִזּוּז וְגִבּוֹר), the LORD mighty in battle (גִּבּוֹר מִלְחָמָה)." Here *gibbor* is explicitly a battle term applied to YHWH as divine warrior.

- **Zephaniah 3:17** — "The LORD your God is in your midst, a גִּבּוֹר who saves (גִּבּוֹר יוֹשִׁיעַ)." The mighty warrior God is here portrayed not as destroying enemies but as saving and rejoicing over his people with singing.

## Theological significance

The word *gibbor* operates on a spectrum from human to divine, and that spectrum is theologically intentional in the OT.

At the human level, *gibbor* acknowledges that physical strength and martial prowess are real and consequential. The OT does not spiritualize away the reality of warfare. David's *gibborim* are celebrated for genuine courage and skill.

At the divine level, the same word applied to God (especially in the compound אֵל גִּבּוֹר) asserts that YHWH possesses the ultimate version of what human warriors approximate. When Psalm 24 asks "Who is this King of glory?" and answers "The LORD, *gibbor* in battle," it claims that the decisive warrior in Israel's story is not David or Joshua but YHWH himself.

The pivot point is Isaiah 9:6, where a human child receives a divine title. The messianic king will be *El Gibbor* — not merely a mighty man but Mighty God. This creates a theological tension that the OT does not fully resolve: how can a human child bear a divine name? The NT identifies Jesus as that resolution, but the OT leaves the tension standing.

The *gibborim* of Genesis 6:4 represent the dark side of the spectrum: strength without submission to God becomes corruption. Nimrod's *gibbor* status is similarly ambiguous. The OT consistently distinguishes between might exercised under divine commission (Joshua, David, Gideon) and might exercised in autonomy or rebellion (Nimrod, the pre-flood *gibborim*, Goliath). The word itself is morally neutral; the narrative context supplies the evaluation.

## Related words

- **גָּבַר** (*gavar*, H1396) — verb root, "to prevail, be mighty"
- **גֶּבֶר** (*gever*, H1397) — "man" (strong man)
- **גְּבוּרָה** (*gevurah*, H1369) — "strength, might, mighty deed"
- **גַּבְרִיאֵל** (*Gavriel*, H1403) — "Gabriel" (mighty one of God)
- **חַיִל** (*chayil*, H2428) — "valor, strength, wealth, army" — see companion study; the two words combine in the phrase גִּבּוֹר חַיִל
- **עִזּוּז** (*izzuz*, H5808) — "mighty, fierce" — paired with *gibbor* in Psalm 24:8
- **אַבִּיר** (*abbir*, H46) — "mighty one, bull" — used of God (Genesis 49:24, "the Mighty One of Jacob") and of powerful humans/animals; a near-synonym but with different connotations (immovability, massiveness)
