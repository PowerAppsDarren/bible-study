# chayil (חַיִל)

**Strong's:** H2428
**Root:** ח-י-ל (*ch-y-l*) — related to the verb חוּל / חִיל (*chul/chil*, H2342), "to writhe, to be in labor, to be strong, to tremble"
**Part of speech:** Noun, masculine
**Glosses:** strength, might, valor, army, wealth, capability, virtue, force, efficiency

## Root and etymology

The noun חַיִל derives from a root that carries the idea of **force exerted under pressure** — the verb חוּל (*chul*) means to twist, writhe, whirl, or tremble, and by extension to be strong (strength as the capacity to endure strain). This is worth noting but must not be overplayed: by the time *chayil* is in common use, its meanings are established by usage, not by etymological reconstruction.

The noun itself is one of the most semantically versatile words in Biblical Hebrew. It covers a range that in English requires at least five different words: strength, valor, wealth, army, and moral capability. The unifying thread is **capacity** — the resources (physical, military, economic, or moral) that enable a person or group to act effectively.

## Semantic range

חַיִל has at least five distinguishable senses, though they shade into one another:

### 1. Military strength / army / fighting force
The most frequent sense. חַיִל can mean the army itself (as a collective noun), or the military capability of an individual or nation.
- "Pharaoh's חַיִל" = Pharaoh's army/force (Exodus 14:4, 9, 17, 28)
- "men of חַיִל" = soldiers, fighting men (Joshua 1:14; 2 Kings 25:5)
- אַנְשֵׁי חַיִל (*anshey chayil*) = "men of capability" in a military context (Exodus 18:21, 25)
- This is the sense behind the common phrase גִּבּוֹר חַיִל (*gibbor chayil*) = "mighty man of valor" = warrior of proven strength

### 2. Strength / power / might (of an individual)
Physical or personal capacity apart from specifically military use.
- "Do you have the חַיִל to..." = do you have the strength/power to...
- Sometimes parallel with כֹּחַ (*koach*, "strength") — the two are near-synonyms in some contexts but *chayil* implies tested, demonstrated capability while *koach* is more raw power

### 3. Wealth / resources / substance
A significant minority of uses. Since wealth is economic power — the capacity to act in the material world — the extension from "strength" to "wealth" is natural in Hebrew thought.
- "a man of חַיִל" in Ruth 2:1 (describing Boaz) may mean "a man of wealth/substance" or "a man of standing" — the ambiguity is likely intentional
- Deuteronomy 8:17-18: "My power and the might of my hand have gotten me this חַיִל" — here clearly "wealth"
- Proverbs 13:22: "the wealth (חַיִל) of the sinner is stored up for the righteous"
- Numbers 31:9: the חַיִל of Midian = their livestock and goods (plundered wealth)

### 4. Moral capability / virtue / excellence
This sense appears primarily in descriptions of character — a person of חַיִל is competent, reliable, morally strong.
- Exodus 18:21: Jethro tells Moses to select אַנְשֵׁי חַיִל (*anshey chayil*), "men of capability" — here meaning competent, trustworthy administrators (the context adds "who fear God, men of truth, hating dishonest gain")
- Ruth 3:11: "all the gate of my people knows that you are אֵשֶׁת חַיִל (*eshet chayil*), a woman of excellence/valor"
- Proverbs 31:10: "An אֵשֶׁת חַיִל (*eshet chayil*), who can find?" — the "woman of valor" whose description fills Proverbs 31:10-31

### 5. Efficiency / ability / competence
A more general sense: the capacity to perform well in a given role.
- 1 Kings 1:42, 52: Adonijah says of Jonathan, "you are אִישׁ חַיִל (*ish chayil*), a man of worth" — capable, reliable
- Genesis 47:6: "if you know אַנְשֵׁי חַיִל (*anshey chayil*) among them" — men of competence (to manage livestock)

**Distribution:** חַיִל occurs approximately 243 times in the OT. It is one of the most common nouns in Biblical Hebrew. The military sense dominates quantitatively, but the wealth and virtue senses are theologically and literarily important beyond their frequency.

## Key occurrences

### The phrase גִּבּוֹר חַיִל (gibbor chayil) — "mighty man of valor"

This compound phrase occurs approximately 37 times in the OT. It combines the proven-warrior sense of *gibbor* with the tested-capability sense of *chayil*. The result is emphatic: not just a strong man, and not just a capable one, but a man whose might has been demonstrated and whose capacity is established.

- **Judges 6:12** — "The LORD is with you, גִּבּוֹר הֶחָיִל (*gibbor hechayil*)." The angel's greeting to Gideon. Note the definite article on *chayil* — see the combined-phrase section below. Gideon is hiding in a winepress. The title is declarative: God calls things that are not as though they are (cf. Romans 4:17).

- **Judges 11:1** — "Now Jephthah the Gileadite was a גִּבּוֹר חַיִל (*gibbor chayil*), and he was the son of a prostitute." The narrator establishes Jephthah's warrior credentials in the same breath as his social stigma. He has *chayil* — military capability — despite lacking social standing. The juxtaposition is deliberate: *chayil* as valor does not require noble birth.

- **Ruth 2:1** — "Now Naomi had a relative of her husband's, a man of standing, אִישׁ גִּבּוֹר חַיִל (*ish gibbor chayil*), of the clan of Elimelech, and his name was Boaz." Here the phrase likely carries the wealth/standing sense more than the military sense. Boaz is introduced as a man of substance and social weight — he has land, workers, resources, and reputation.

- **Ruth 3:11** — "All the gate of my people knows that you are an אֵשֶׁת חַיִל (*eshet chayil*), a woman of valor." Boaz speaks this to Ruth. The masculine military term is reframed in the feminine: Ruth's *chayil* is her demonstrated character — loyalty, courage, initiative, moral strength. She left her homeland, clung to Naomi, worked the fields, and acted with integrity. The word choice is intentional: the narrator has already called Boaz an *ish gibbor chayil* (2:1), and now Boaz recognizes Ruth as his female counterpart.

- **Proverbs 31:10** — "An אֵשֶׁת חַיִל (*eshet chayil*), who can find? Her worth is far above rubies." The acrostic poem that follows (31:10-31) defines *chayil* in the feminine through a catalogue of competencies: economic productivity (vv. 13-19, 24), household management (vv. 15, 21, 27), generosity (v. 20), wisdom (v. 26), fear of the LORD (v. 30). This is the fullest unpacking of *chayil* as virtue/capability in the OT. The poem answers its own question: this is what a woman of *chayil* looks like in practice.

- **1 Samuel 9:1** — "There was a man of Benjamin, whose name was Kish... a גִּבּוֹר חַיִל (*gibbor chayil*)." Saul's father is introduced with this phrase. The sense is "a man of standing/substance" — wealth and social position. This is preparation for Saul's introduction: the future king comes from an established family.

- **2 Kings 5:1** — "Now Naaman, commander of the army of the king of Aram, was a great man before his master and highly honored... He was also a גִּבּוֹר חַיִל (*gibbor chayil*). But he was a leper." As with Jephthah, the narrator pairs the high status (*gibbor chayil*) with a disqualifying condition. Naaman has military might, social standing, and royal favor — but leprosy undoes it all. The structure is deliberate: *chayil* cannot solve everything.

- **Joshua 1:14** — "All the גִּבּוֹרֵי הַחַיִל (*gibborey hachayil*) shall cross over armed before your brothers." Military sense, straightforward: the capable warriors of the Transjordan tribes must lead the invasion. Note the definite article: THE men of valor — the specific, identified fighting force.

- **1 Chronicles 12:1-22** — David's warriors at Ziklag are called גִּבּוֹרִים עֹזְרֵי הַמִּלְחָמָה (*gibborim ozrey hammilchamah*, "mighty men, helpers in war," v. 1) and גִּבּוֹרֵי חַיִל (*gibborey chayil*, vv. 21, 25, 28, 30). The chapter is a catalogue of *chayil* in its military sense — men who defected to David and proved their worth.

- **Exodus 18:21, 25** — Jethro advises Moses: "Choose אַנְשֵׁי חַיִל (*anshey chayil*) from all the people — God-fearing, trustworthy men who hate dishonest gain." Here *chayil* is administrative competence combined with moral integrity. The military sense is absent; the emphasis is on character fitness for leadership.

- **Deuteronomy 8:17-18** — "You may say in your heart, 'My power (כֹּחִי, *kochi*) and the strength of my hand have made me this חַיִל (wealth).' But remember the LORD your God, for it is he who gives you the power (כֹּחַ) to make חַיִל (wealth)." Here *chayil* unmistakably means wealth/resources, and the passage warns against the illusion that human *chayil* is self-generated.

- **Psalm 118:15-16** — "The right hand of the LORD does חַיִל (*chayil*, 'valiantly')." Three times the phrase is repeated. Here *chayil* is a verb-like use (עָשָׂה חַיִל, *asah chayil*, "to do/make valor" = to act with power). Applied to God, *chayil* ascends to its highest register.

## Theological significance

חַיִל is theologically important because it refuses to separate what modern Western categories divide:

**Strength and virtue are the same word.** In Hebrew thought, the "woman of valor" (Proverbs 31) and the "mighty warrior" (Judges 6:12) share a single vocabulary because moral strength and physical/economic strength are variations of the same underlying reality: the capacity to act effectively in the world. *Chayil* insists that courage on the battlefield and diligence in the household are species of the same genus.

**Wealth and military strength are the same word.** This is not confusion but realism. In the ancient world, economic resources and military capacity were inseparable — armies required wealth, and wealth required defense. *Chayil* holds together what later abstractions pull apart.

**The word is morally neutral until contextualized.** *Chayil* can describe the army of Pharaoh pursuing Israel (Exodus 14) or the right hand of the LORD (Psalm 118). The word names a capacity; the narrative evaluates how that capacity is used.

**Deuteronomy 8:17-18 is the theological fulcrum:** all *chayil* — whether military, economic, or personal — ultimately derives from God. The person of *chayil* is not self-made; they are God-equipped. This frames the angel's greeting in Judges 6:12: when God calls Gideon *gibbor hechayil*, the implication is not "you already have valor" but "the LORD is with you" — and it is the LORD's presence that constitutes the *chayil*.

**Ruth as *eshet chayil*** is a remarkable literary move. The same language used of warriors and wealthy patriarchs is applied to a destitute foreign widow whose only assets are loyalty and courage. The Book of Ruth thereby redefines *chayil*: it is not fundamentally about swords or silver but about the kind of character that God honors. Proverbs 31 then universalizes this redefinition.

## Related words

- **גִּבּוֹר** (*gibbor*, H1368) — "mighty man, warrior" — see companion study; the two words combine in גִּבּוֹר חַיִל
- **כֹּחַ** (*koach*, H3581) — "strength, power" — near-synonym but emphasizes raw capacity rather than proven, demonstrated might
- **עֹז** (*oz*, H5797) — "strength, might" — often used of God's strength; more abstract than *chayil*
- **חֵיל** (*cheyl*, H2426) — "rampart, outer wall" — same consonants, different vowels, different word; a fortification. Some scholars see a conceptual link (the army as a "wall" of defense) but the etymological connection is uncertain.
- **צָבָא** (*tsava*, H6635) — "army, host" — the standard word for an organized military force (cf. "LORD of hosts," יהוה צְבָאוֹת); *chayil* as "army" emphasizes capability while *tsava* emphasizes organized deployment
- **אֵשֶׁת חַיִל** (*eshet chayil*) — "woman of valor/excellence" — not a separate lemma but a fixed phrase with its own semantic identity (Ruth 3:11; Proverbs 12:4; 31:10)
