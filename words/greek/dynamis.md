# dynamis (δύναμις)

**Strong's:** G1411
**Part of speech:** Noun, feminine, 3rd declension
**Glosses:** power, might, capability, miracle, mighty work, army/force

## Semantic range

δύναμις is the central "power" word in Koine Greek and covers a range comparable to Hebrew חַיִל (*chayil*):

1. **Power / might / capability** — inherent ability to accomplish something. "The δύναμις of God" = God's power to act (Romans 1:16; 1 Corinthians 1:18, 24).

2. **Miracle / mighty work** — a specific manifestation of supernatural power. Often in the plural: δυνάμεις = "mighty works, miracles" (Matthew 11:20-23; Mark 6:2; Acts 2:22). The word does not merely mean "wonder" (τέρας, *teras*) but emphasizes the power behind the event.

3. **Force / army** — a military or cosmic power. In the LXX, δύναμις frequently translates חַיִל (*chayil*) in its "army" sense, and צָבָא (*tsava*, "host") in the phrase "LORD of hosts" (κύριος τῶν δυνάμεων). In the NT, "the powers of the heavens" (αἱ δυνάμεις τῶν οὐρανῶν, Matthew 24:29) may refer to cosmic forces or angelic armies.

4. **Spiritual power / enabling** — the Holy Spirit's empowerment of believers. Acts 1:8: "You will receive δύναμιν when the Holy Spirit comes upon you." This is the NT equivalent of the angel's declaration to Gideon: divine presence produces divine capability.

## Key occurrences

- **Luke 1:35** — "The δύναμις of the Most High will overshadow you." The power that accomplishes the incarnation.
- **Luke 4:14** — Jesus returns to Galilee "in the δύναμις of the Spirit." His ministry operates by Spirit-given power.
- **Acts 1:8** — "You will receive δύναμιν when the Holy Spirit has come upon you." The programmatic statement for the book of Acts.
- **Romans 1:16** — "The gospel is the δύναμις of God for salvation." The gospel is not merely information but divine power.
- **1 Corinthians 1:18, 24** — "The word of the cross is... the δύναμις of God." Christ crucified is God's power — a paradox (power in apparent weakness).
- **2 Corinthians 12:9** — "My δύναμις is made perfect in weakness." The Gideon pattern restated: divine power flows through human inadequacy.
- **Ephesians 1:19-21** — Paul stacks four power-words: δύναμις, ἐνέργεια (*energeia*), κράτος (*kratos*), ἰσχύς (*ischys*) — to describe the power God exercised in raising Christ, a power now available to believers.
- **Ephesians 3:20** — "Him who is able to do far more abundantly... according to the δύναμις that works within us."
- **2 Timothy 1:7** — "God gave us a spirit not of fear but of δύναμις and love and self-control."

## Theological significance

δύναμις in the NT consistently points to power that originates in God and is mediated to humans through the Spirit. The pattern established in the OT — where חַיִל (*chayil*) belongs to God and is bestowed on those he commissions — continues in the NT with δύναμις as its Greek vehicle.

The word resists the modern split between "spiritual" and "practical" power. In Acts, δύναμις produces both miracles (Acts 3:12) and bold proclamation (Acts 4:33). In Paul, it is both the content of the gospel (Romans 1:16) and the experience of the believer (Ephesians 3:20). The OT background in *chayil* — which seamlessly covered military, economic, and moral capacity — explains why the NT writers use δύναμις with the same breadth.

## Related words

- **δυνατός** (*dynatos*, G1415) — adjective, "powerful, mighty, able" — the LXX's primary rendering of גִּבּוֹר (*gibbor*)
- **δυνάστης** (*dynastes*, G1413) — "ruler, sovereign, potentate" (Luke 1:52; Acts 8:27; 1 Timothy 6:15)
- **δύναμαι** (*dynamai*, G1410) — verb, "to be able, to have power"
- **ἐνδυναμόω** (*endynamoo*, G1743) — verb, "to strengthen, to empower" — used in Ephesians 6:10 ("be strong in the Lord") and Philippians 4:13 ("I can do all things through him who strengthens me")
- **ἰσχύς** (*ischys*, G2479) — "strength, might" — see companion study
- **κράτος** (*kratos*, G2904) — "dominion, ruling power, might"
- **ἐξουσία** (*exousia*, G1849) — "authority, right" — distinguished from δύναμις as authorized right vs. inherent ability

## LXX background

δύναμις translates primarily:
- חַיִל (*chayil*, H2428) — in its "army" and "strength" senses
- צָבָא (*tsava*, H6635) — "host, army" (especially in "LORD of hosts")
- גְּבוּרָה (*gevurah*, H1369) — "might, mighty deed"
- כֹּחַ (*koach*, H3581) — "power, strength"

This LXX breadth means that when a first-century reader encountered δύναμις in a letter from Paul, the word carried echoes of the entire OT power vocabulary.
