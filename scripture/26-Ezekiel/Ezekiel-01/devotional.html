Ezekiel 1:1–3 · The setting

By the River, Among the Captives

Before a single wheel turns, the book nails itself to a map and a calendar. The where and the when are the whole shock. Ezekiel isn't standing in the Jerusalem temple, where God's glory was understood to dwell over the ark. He is a deportee in Mesopotamia, by an irrigation canal, on the wrong side of the world.

"Now it came about in the thirtieth year… while I was by the river Chebar among those who had been made prisoners, that the heavens were made open and I saw visions of God."
Ezekiel 1:1 · BBE

The date is given twice over: "the fifth day of the month, in the fifth year after King Jehoiachin had been made a prisoner" (v.2). Jehoiachin was carried off to Babylon in 597 BC, so his fifth year lands the vision around 593 BC — Ezekiel among the first wave of exiles, while Jerusalem and its temple still stood, six years from the fire of 586. The "thirtieth year" of verse 1 is debated: the oldest and most common reading is that this is Ezekiel's own age — thirty, the year a priest entered full temple service (Numbers 4:3). The grim irony is built in: at the age he should have begun serving at the altar, he is a priest with no temple, hundreds of miles away.

The river Chebar is the Kebar canal (Akkadian nār Kabari), a major waterway off the Euphrates near the city of Nippur in southern Babylonia, where a community of Judean exiles was settled. Babylonian administrative tablets actually mention this canal — the geography is real, not symbolic. And there, in unclean Gentile territory, "the heavens were made open." The glory that lived behind the veil came looking for the people in chains.

Ezekiel 1:4 · The approach

A Storm Out of the North

"And, looking, I saw a storm-wind coming out of the north, a great cloud with flames of fire coming after one another, and a bright light shining round about it and in the heart of it was something coloured like electrum."
Ezekiel 1:4 · BBE

The vision opens like weather — wind, cloud, fire, lightning, the storm-language of God's appearings all through the Old Testament (Exodus 19, Psalm 18, Nahum 1). But note the direction: out of the north. To an exile, the north was the road every invading army took into the land — Babylon's own armies came down from the north. Ezekiel watches the storm roll in from the very direction of judgment, and in its glowing heart is something the translators have wrestled with for two thousand years.

The words the vision rests on

Four Hebrew Words That Carry It

Ezekiel's vision is famously hard to picture, and part of the reason is the vocabulary. A few load-bearing words are either rare, technical, or deliberately uncertain — and where the Hebrew is genuinely unsure, honesty matters more than a confident gloss.

חַשְׁמַל
chashmal
gleaming amber · electrum (?)

The "electrum" of v.4 and v.27. Chashmal appears only three times in the whole Bible — all in Ezekiel — so there's no other context to pin it down. Ancient translators guessed: the Greek read it as elektron (amber or gold-silver alloy), the Latin as a glowing bronze. The honest answer is that no one is certain what it means — a radiant, fiery, metallic sheen is as far as the evidence safely reaches. (In Modern Hebrew the same word was later borrowed to mean "electricity.")

חַיּוֹת
chayyot
living creatures · living ones

BBE's "living beings" (v.5). The root is chai, "alive" — these are, simply, "the living ones." Ezekiel doesn't yet name them; he only describes. It's not until the parallel vision of Ezekiel 10:20 that he tells us plainly: "This is the living being… by the river Chebar; and I was certain that they were the winged ones" — the cherubim, the throne-guardians woven over the ark and the veil.

אוֹפַן
ophan
wheel

The ordinary word for a cart or chariot wheel (v.15–16). Its plainness is the point: this is a chariot. What Ezekiel sees is the throne of God made mobile — fitted with wheels, able to go anywhere. Later Jewish tradition named the whole vision the Merkavah, "the Chariot," and treated it as the most awesome and guarded passage in Scripture.

רָקִיעַ
raqia
expanse · firmament

BBE softens it to "arch" (v.22); WEB keeps "expanse." It is the same word as Genesis 1:6 — the raqia God spread over the waters of creation. Ezekiel is borrowing the architecture of Genesis: above the creatures stretches a crystalline dome, and above the dome, a throne. The vision is a creation, in miniature, with God enthroned over it.

PS
Perry Stone

Reads Ezekiel through its Hebrew roots and the temple furniture, and he'd point to the chayyot as the living version of what was only ever embroidered and carved before. Cherubim were woven into the veil and spread over the mercy seat — Ezekiel is shown the breathing reality the temple art was always pointing at. The same guardians that stood watch at Eden's gate (Genesis 3:24) attend the throne that has come to find the exiles.

Ezekiel 1:5–14 · The living creatures

Four Faces, Four Directions

Each living creature has four faces and four wings, feet like a calf's gleaming like polished brass, and human hands beneath the wings. They move as one, never turning — whichever way they face, they already face forward — darting and returning like lightning. And the four faces are named:

"…they had the face of a man, and the four of them had the face of a lion on the right side, and the four of them had the face of an ox on the left side, and the four of them had the face of an eagle."
Ezekiel 1:10 · BBE

The four are often read as the whole of creation gathered around the throne — the chief of the wild beasts (lion), of the tame (ox), of the birds (eagle), and the crown of creation (man) — every order of living thing represented before God. Between the creatures burns coal-fire, and lightning runs out of it. This is a living throne, and all that lives is bound up in its service.

CM
Chuck Missler

Loved structural patterns, and he'd connect these four faces to a reading as old as the early church: Irenaeus and Jerome matched the four faces to the four Gospels — the lion (the King, Matthew), the ox (the Servant, Mark), the man (the Son of Man, Luke), and the eagle (the soaring divine Word, John). It's an ancient interpretation, not a claim Ezekiel makes himself — but the early readers heard the same fourfold portrait of one figure echoing forward.

Ezekiel 1:15–21 · The wheels

A Wheel Inside a Wheel

"The form of the wheels and their work was like a beryl… and they were like a wheel inside a wheel… And I saw that they had edges, and their edges, even of the four, were full of eyes round about."
Ezekiel 1:16, 18 · BBE

Beside each creature stands a wheel gleaming like a yellow-green gem, and each wheel is somehow two wheels set at right angles — "a wheel inside a wheel." The effect is a wheel that can roll in any of the four directions without ever turning, matching the creatures who already face every way at once. The throne can go anywhere, instantly, in any direction. Nothing about it is fixed in place.

And the rims are full of eyes, all the way around. The ancient throne-chariot sees in every direction it can travel. In the parallel vision the wheels even get a name of their own — in Ezekiel 10:13 a voice calls out to them galgal (גַּלְגַּל), "the whirling thing." The deepest detail is the engine: the wheels don't roll on their own.

"Wherever the spirit was to go they went… for the spirit of the living beings was in the wheels."
Ezekiel 1:20 · BBE

Creatures and wheels rise, halt, and dart as one because a single ruach — spirit, breath, wind — drives the whole assembly. This vast machine of fire has one will moving through it. It is not many powers; it is one Presence, going wherever it intends.

💎
Ezekiel 1:22–26 · The throne

The Crystal Dome and the Sapphire Seat

The eye is led upward in stages. Over the creatures' heads stretches the raqia, the expanse, shining like terrible ice (v.22). Their wings, in motion, roar "like the sound of great waters… like the rushing of an army" (v.24) — and then they fall still, and a voice comes from above the dome. Above the crystal, the climb reaches its summit:

"And on the top of the arch which was over their heads was the form of a king's seat, like a sapphire stone; and on the form of the seat was the form of a man seated on it on high."
Ezekiel 1:26 · BBE

A throne of sapphire — the same blue stone under God's feet when the elders of Israel saw Him on Sinai, "as it were a paved work of a sapphire stone" (Exodus 24:10). And on it, "the form of a man." Here Ezekiel's language goes deliberately, reverently vague — and that vagueness is the point of the next section.

The summit of the vision

"The Likeness of the Glory of the LORD"

Featured Verse · Ezekiel 1:28
הוּא מַרְאֵה דְּמוּת כְּבוֹד־יְהוָה
hu mar'eh demut kevod-YHWH — "this was the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the LORD"
"Like the bow in the cloud on a day of rain, so was the light shining round him. And this is what the glory of the Lord was like. And when I saw it I went down on my face, and the voice of one talking came to my ears."
Ezekiel 1:28 · BBE

Read the climb again and listen to the grammar. Ezekiel never says he saw God. He stacks buffer-words — over and over he writes "the likeness… as the appearance of… something like…" The closing line of the chapter piles three of them in a row: it was not the glory, but the appearance of the likeness of the glory. Two Hebrew words do the buffering:

דְּמוּת
demut
likeness · resemblance

Not the thing itself, but its resemblance. The same word as Genesis 1:26, where humanity is made in God's demut. Ezekiel uses it relentlessly here — a throne's demut, a man's demut — to insist that he is describing a likeness, never the bare reality.

מַרְאֶה
mar'eh
appearance · what is seen

"The look of," the visible surface. Paired with demut it doubles the distance: the appearance of the likeness of the glory. Ezekiel strains language to its limit precisely so we don't mistake the picture for a photograph. Reverence guards every line.

The whole towering vision resolves not into a definition but into a kavod — the weight, the glory of the LORD — crowned with a rainbow, the sign of God's covenant mercy set in the storm-cloud (Genesis 9). And the only possible response to it is the one Ezekiel gives: he falls on his face. The chariot of fire that crossed the world to reach the exiles ends in a bow of mercy and a man face-down in the dirt.

The vision, echoed across Scripture

Where Else This Throne Appears

Ezekiel's throne-vision is one of the most quoted images in the Bible's own imagination. Tracing the echoes shows how carefully it is woven into the rest of Scripture.

Revelation 4 stands in the same throne-room — four living creatures, full of eyes, around a throne ringed with a rainbow:

"…round about the throne, were four living creatures full of eyes before and behind… the first creature like a lion, the second like a calf, the third with a face like a man, the fourth like an eagle."
Revelation 4:6–7 · paraphrase of WEB

The four faces are the same four — lion, ox, man, eagle — and there is a rainbow about the throne (Revelation 4:3), just as in Ezekiel 1:28. But John sees a difference worth catching: Ezekiel's creatures each have all four faces, while in Revelation each creature wears a single face, the four distributed among them. Same fourfold worship before the throne, arranged a different way — John is clearly drawing on Ezekiel, not merely copying him.

And this vision is the hinge of the whole book of Ezekiel. The point of putting God's throne on wheels becomes devastatingly clear later: in chapters 10–11 Ezekiel watches the kavod, the glory, rise up and leave the Jerusalem temple — pausing at the threshold, then the east gate, then departing to the mountain east of the city (Ezekiel 10:18–19; 11:23). The glory that is mobile can go — and it does, abandoning the temple to judgment. Yet the same book ends with the glory returning from the east to a new temple (Ezekiel 43:1–5). Chapter 1 is the vision that makes both the leaving and the returning possible: God's presence was never bolted to one building.

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Teaches from the Hebrew under the text, and kavod is a word he'd open up: it comes from a root meaning "heavy, weighty." The glory of God is His weight — the felt, crushing reality of His presence. The staggering thing in Ezekiel is where that weight shows up: not in the holy city but in Babylon, by a canal, among prisoners. The weighty Presence went into exile with His people.

One more thread, handled with care. Christian readers across the centuries have lingered on "the likeness as the appearance of a man" enthroned in fire (v.26), reading it alongside Daniel's "one like a son of man" (Daniel 7:13) and the radiant figure of Revelation 1 — a glimpse, they argue, of God appearing in human form. This is interpretation, offered reverently, not a claim the text spells out. Jewish tradition reads the merkavah very differently and historically fenced it off as too holy for casual study. Ezekiel himself only gives us the buffered "likeness" — and where readers and traditions differ here, the honest move is to name the views and let the prophet's own restraint stand.

Where the vision lands

Closing Reflection

Ezekiel 1 is a doorway into the strangest and most visual book of the prophets, and it hands every reader three things to carry forward:

The throne has wheels

God's glory is not the property of one nation or one building. Fitted with wheels and full of eyes, the throne can go anywhere — and it went to Babylon. No exile is past its reach.

One will moves it all

Creatures, fire, and wheels rise and halt together because one spirit drives them. The vast machinery of heaven is not chaos; it is a single Presence going exactly where it intends.

It ends in a rainbow

The storm out of the north resolves into a bow of covenant mercy and a man face-down before it. The proper response to the weight of God is not analysis but worship.

A captive priest, robbed of his temple, looks up from a foreign canal — and discovers that the God he thought he'd left behind in Jerusalem has come the whole distance to find him. The glory is not bound to a building; it goes where its people are. That is the truth the whole book of Ezekiel will spend forty-eight chapters proving.

Next · Ezekiel 2

"Son of man, stand on your feet"

The vision was never the point — the commission was. Flat on his face before the glory, Ezekiel hears a voice lift him up and send him: to a rebellious house, with hard words, whether they listen or not. The throne that crossed the world to reach the exiles now has a messenger to send back to them. The chariot of fire becomes a calling.
