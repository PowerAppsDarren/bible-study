Psalm 7 · The Title

A Wild Song Over a Whispered Lie

Most psalms wear a small caption above the first verse — a tune, a name, an occasion. Psalm 7's is one of the strangest in the book: "Shiggaion of David, which he sang unto the LORD, concerning the words of Cush the Benjamite" (KJV). Two riddles sit in that single line, and both shape how the poem reads.

The first is the word Shiggaion. It is a musical or literary label that appears only here and (in plural) at Habakkuk 3:1 — so rare that no one is certain what it means. The leading guess ties it to the root shagah, "to reel, to wander, to stagger," which would make a shiggaion a wild, lurching, impassioned song — irregular in meter, sung in the heat of distress. Other scholars connect it to a "lament" or a "wandering" melody. The honest summary: it signals strong feeling, and the rest is uncertain.

The second riddle is Cush the Benjamite — a man named nowhere else in Scripture. Because he is "of Benjamin," Saul's tribe, the old reading is that this is one of Saul's partisans, slandering David during the years he was hunted. Whoever Cush was, the title pins the psalm to "the words of" him — a man whose weapon was his mouth. This is a prayer about being lied about, and not being able to outrun it.

BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Reads the Bible as a unified literary work, genre by genre, and he'd name this one first: Psalm 7 is an individual lament that swells into an imprecatory psalm — a cry for justice against an enemy. The genre has a shape, and Psalm 7 follows it almost like a courtroom transcript: complaint, plea of innocence, appeal to the Judge, verdict, praise. Knowing the genre tells you this isn't private revenge — it is a man refusing to take the sword himself and laying the whole case before the bench of heaven.

🦁
Psalm 7:1–2

The Lion at the Throat

"O Lord my God, I put my faith in you; take me out of the hands of him who is cruel to me, and make me free; so that he may not come rushing on my soul like a lion, wounding it, while there is no one to be my saviour."
Psalm 7:1–2 · BBE

The psalm opens not with the accusation but with a direction: "I put my faith in you." The Hebrew verb is chasah — to take refuge, to run for cover, the way a small animal bolts into a cleft of rock when the hawk's shadow falls. It's the same verb that ends Psalm 2: "blessed are all who take refuge in Him." Before David describes the danger, he names the rock he is running to.

And the danger is vivid: a lion, rushing in to tear the soul "while there is no one to be my saviour." This is the terror of the slandered — the sense of being hunted alone, with no human ally able to call off the predator. The only deliverer left is the one he has already run to. The refuge comes first, on purpose, before the fear is even spoken.

✋
Psalm 7:3–5

The Oath of Clean Hands

Now the psalm does something startling. The accused man invites God to check — and stakes his own life on the result. This is an oath of innocence, the ancient form of a defendant swearing himself clear by calling down the curse if he lies:

"O Lord my God, if I have done this; if my hands have done any wrong; if I have given back evil to him who did evil to me… Let my hater go after my soul and take it; let my life be crushed to the earth, and my honour into the dust. (Selah.)"
Psalm 7:3–5 · BBE

Read the logic: "if I really did what they say — then let the lion win." He is so certain of his innocence in this matter that he is willing to forfeit life and honor if he's lying. Note the careful limit — he doesn't claim to be sinless before God; he claims clean hands toward this accuser, including the telling detail that he spared an enemy who had no claim on mercy (v.4). It is a specific plea about a specific charge.

Then comes Selah — a musical or liturgical pause whose exact function is, like Shiggaion, debated; most take it as a marked beat to stop and weigh what was just sung. Here it falls perfectly: a man has just put his neck on the line. Let the silence hold.

🏛️
Psalm 7:6–8

"Arise, and Take the Bench"

With clean hands sworn, the plea turns into a summons. David calls God to rise — to stop seeming absent and convene the court:

"Come up, Lord, in your wrath; be lifted up against my haters; be awake, my God, give orders for the judging. The meeting of the nations will be round you; take your seat, then, over them, on high."
Psalm 7:6–7 · BBE

The scene zooms out from one slanderer to a courtroom the size of the world. "The meeting of the nations" gathers; God "takes his seat… on high" — the posture of a judge mounting the bench. A private grievance has become a cosmic assize, and the accused asks not to be avenged by his own hand but to be vindicated by a verdict: "give a decision for me, O Lord, because of my righteousness" (v.8). He wants a ruling, not a riot.

🔥
The verse the whole psalm turns on

"The God Who Tries Hearts and Reins"

Featured Verse · Psalm 7:9
בֹּחֵן לִבּוֹת וּכְלָיוֹת
bochen libbot u-khelayot — testing the hearts and the kidneys
"O let the evil of the evil-doer come to an end, but give strength to the upright: for men's minds and hearts are tested by the God of righteousness."
Psalm 7:9 · BBE

Here is why an innocent man can dare to invite God's inspection: the Judge does not weigh reputation; He weighs the inside. Two Hebrew words carry the claim.

בָּחַן
bachan
to test · assay · examine

Not a casual glance. Bachan is the metalworker's word — to assay ore, to test gold and silver in the fire to see what is real and what is dross. God does not merely see the heart; He refines and proves it, separating the genuine from the counterfeit. The slandered man is asking for exactly this furnace.

כְּלָיוֹת
kelayot
kidneys · "reins" · the inmost self

Literally the kidneys — the older translations say "reins." To the Hebrew mind the kidneys were the seat of the deepest emotions and conscience, the place no one else can reach. Paired with lev, "heart" (the will and mind), the phrase means God searches you all the way down — past the public face, past even what you can see in yourself.

This is the engine of the psalm. A man cannot prove his innocence to a crowd that has already believed the lie — but he can hand his hidden self to the one Judge who reads the reins. The verdict that matters is rendered where the slander can't follow.

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Teaches from the Hebrew beneath the English, and he'd linger on those "reins." We read "God tries the hearts and minds" and picture thought; the Hebrew says hearts and kidneys — the most hidden organ, the part of you you never see. The point is total exposure and total knowledge: there is no inner room God hasn't entered. For the falsely accused that is the most comforting sentence in the psalm — the only opinion that finally counts belongs to the One who already knows everything.

🔗
"He who searches the reins" across Scripture

The Searcher of Hearts, Verse by Verse

The image of God testing heart and reins is not unique to Psalm 7 — it becomes a fixed thread, running from the Law to the Prophets to the last book of the Bible. Tracing it shows how settled this picture of God was.

Jeremiah twice makes it God's signature. Wronged like David, the prophet prays: "O Lord… who tries the reins and the heart, let me see your vengeance on them" (Jeremiah 11:20) — almost a quotation of Psalm 7. And God says it of Himself: "I the Lord search the heart, I try the reins, even to give every man according to his ways" (Jeremiah 17:10).

"I the Lord search the heart, I try the reins, even to give every man according to the fruit of his doings."
Jeremiah 17:10 · paraphrase of KJV/WEB

The Psalter says it again in Psalm 26:2 — "test my reins and my heart" — and the New Testament hands the very title to the risen Christ. In Revelation 2:23 He declares: "I am he who searches the reins and hearts; and I will give to every one of you according to your works." The phrase David coined under slander becomes, at the end of the Bible, a name for the Judge of all. The God who reads the kidneys in Psalm 7 is the same one walking among the lampstands in Revelation.

Psalm 7:10–13

The Shield, and the Bent Bow

The verdict comes, and it has two faces. To the upright, God is cover; to the unrepentant, God is armed.

מָגֵן
magen
shield · defense

"God… is my breastplate" (BBE); older versions, "my defence" or "my shield." Magen is the small round shield a soldier holds between himself and the blow. For the one who runs to refuge in v.1, the answer in v.10 is that the refuge has a body in front of him — God Himself stands in the path of the arrow.

קֶשֶׁת
qeshet
bow · the bent weapon

The other face. If the wicked "is not turned" (v.12), God "will make his sword sharp; his bow is bent and ready" with "arrows flames of fire" (vv.12–13). The patience is real but not endless — God is "angry with the evil-doers every day" (v.11). The bow is already drawn; what's still open is whether the man will turn.

"God is the judge of the upright, and is angry with the evil-doers every day. If a man is not turned from his evil, he will make his sword sharp; his bow is bent and ready."
Psalm 7:11–12 · BBE

The single word holding the door open is "turned." The armed God is still a God who would rather the wicked turn than fall. The drawn bow is a warning aimed at producing repentance, not just a sentence already passed.

🪃
Psalm 7:14–16

The Pit a Man Digs for Himself

Then the psalm reveals how the wicked fall — and it is the most quietly brilliant turn in the poem. The evil-doer is not struck by a bolt from heaven. He is destroyed by his own scheme, recoiling on him like a thrown weapon. The Hebrew runs the violence through three motions:

🤰
he conceives mischief
⛏️
he digs a deep pit
🕳️
he falls into it himself
"That man is a worker of evil; the seed of wrongdoing has given birth to deceit. He has made a hole deep in the earth, and is falling into the hole which he has made. His wrongdoing will come back to him, and his violent behaviour will come down on his head."
Psalm 7:14–16 · BBE

Verse 14 is a grim pregnancy: the wicked "travails with iniquity… conceives mischief… brings forth falsehood" (KJV). Evil here is something you carry and give birth to — it gestates inside, then comes out into the world as a lie. And verse 15 catches the trapper in his own trap: he digs the pit meant for the innocent, and tumbles into it while it's still warm from his shovel.

This is not luck or irony. It is the moral architecture of the universe as Scripture sees it — sin is self-collapsing. "His wrongdoing will come back to him… come down on his head" (v.16). The harm boomerangs.

CM
Chuck Missler

Loved structural fingerprints like this — the way the punishment is built into the crime, not bolted on afterward. He'd point out that this "dig-a-pit-and-fall-in" motif is one of the most repeated images in the wisdom books, a designed feature of the moral order: God often judges the wicked simply by letting their own plans run to term. The slandered man doesn't have to lift a hand. The trap does the work.

🕳️
The pit that recoils

One Image, Dug All Through Scripture

The boomerang of verses 14–16 is a wisdom proverb the whole Bible keeps repeating. It surfaces in Proverbs, again in the Psalms, in Ecclesiastes — a settled law of the moral world.

"Whoever digs a pit shall fall into it; and he who rolls a stone, it will come back on him."
Proverbs 26:27 · paraphrase of WEB

Psalm 9, just two psalms later, says it almost identically: "The nations have gone down into the hole which they made: in the net which they put secretly is their own foot taken" (Psalm 9:15, BBE) — the same trapper, the same trap. Psalm 57:6 repeats it: "they have made ready a net for my steps… they have made a hole before me, and have gone down into it themselves." And the Preacher distills it to a single line: "He who digs a pit will fall into it" (Ecclesiastes 10:8).

Read together, these passages say Psalm 7 is not voicing a private hope that the bad guy gets his — it is stating a structural truth the sages kept finding everywhere: malice is a pit-digger that ends up underground. The innocent can leave the verdict to the Judge precisely because the trap is already set to spring on its maker.

🎵
Psalm 7:17

The Song at the End of the Trial

"I will give praise to the Lord for his righteousness; I will make a song to the name of the Lord Most High."
Psalm 7:17 · BBE

The wild, lurching shiggaion ends in a steady note of praise — and notice what it praises. Not "for rescuing me," not "for destroying my enemy," but "for his righteousness." The thing David celebrates is that the Judge is just. The whole psalm has been an appeal to God's character, and the closing line rests the case on it.

The last title is El Elyon, "the Lord Most High" — God over the nations that gathered in verse 7, enthroned above the whole assize. A song that began staggering under a lie lands on the highest, steadiest name in the book. The man who could not clear his name has found the Name that clears it.

🕊️
What the psalm hands you

Closing Reflection

Psalm 7 is the prayer of anyone who has ever been lied about and could not catch the lie. It hands the reader three things to carry:

Run before you argue

The psalm names the refuge in verse 1, before it names the danger in verse 2. The first move of the falsely accused isn't a defense — it's a direction: chasah, run for cover in God.

Let the Judge read the reins

You can't prove your heart to a crowd that's already decided. But the one verdict that counts is rendered by the God who tries hearts and reins — where slander cannot reach.

Leave the pit to dig itself

You don't have to spring the trap. Malice is a pit-digger that falls in its own hole. Vengeance left in God's hands is not weakness — it's the moral order working.

A staggering song, sung over a whispered lie, climbs all the way up to the bench of heaven and comes back down as praise — not because the enemy fell, but because the Judge is righteous. When you cannot clear your name, Psalm 7 points you to the One who can, and to the Court that reads what no one else can see.

Next · Psalm 8

"What is man, that you keep him in mind?"

From the courtroom of Psalm 7 to the night sky of Psalm 8. The slandered man who looked up to the Judge now looks up at the heavens — the moon and the stars God set in place — and asks the question that has stopped every honest soul under that sky: who are we, that the Most High should give us a thought? The trial gives way to wonder.
