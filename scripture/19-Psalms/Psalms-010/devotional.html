Every psalm around it has a title. This one begins bare, with a raw question hurled at the sky — because it isn't a new song at all. It's the second half of one already in progress.

Psalm 10 · The Untitled Half

A Song Without a Superscription

Psalm 9 has a heading — "To the chief Musician… A Psalm of David." Psalm 11 has one. So do nearly all the psalms in this stretch. But Psalm 10 opens with nothing — no author, no tune, no occasion. In a section of the Psalter where titles are the rule, that silence is a clue, not an accident.

Read the two psalms back-to-back and the seam disappears. Psalms 9 and 10 share the same vocabulary, the same enemies, the same heartbeat — and together they run a single alphabet acrostic, each new section opening with the next letter of the Hebrew alphabet. Psalm 9 carries it from aleph roughly to kaph; Psalm 10 picks the thread back up around lamed (ל) and finishes the alphabet at taw. The poem was always one. Hebrew tradition split it; the Greek Old Testament and the Latin Vulgate keep them joined as a single "Psalm 9" — which is why Catholic and Orthodox Bibles number much of the Psalter one behind the Hebrew from here on.

BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Reads the Bible as one unified literary work, and he'd start with the form: an acrostic is a poem built on the alphabet, a way of saying "from A to Z, start to finish." Splitting a single acrostic into "Psalm 9" and "Psalm 10" is like tearing one chapter in two — which is exactly why Psalm 10 reads like it begins in the middle of a thought. Psalm 9 was the praise half; Psalm 10 is the lament half, and the acrostic stitches the joy and the anguish into one fabric.

So the despair of Psalm 10 doesn't float free. It hangs on the praise of Psalm 9 — "I will give thanks… I will be glad" — and that bracket of confidence is the rope this darker half is tied to.

❓
Psalm 10:1

Why Do You Stand Far Off?

"Why do you keep far away, O Lord? why are you not to be seen in times of trouble?"
Psalm 10:1 · BBE

It is one of the most honest opening lines in all of Scripture. Not "the LORD is my shepherd," not "praise the LORD" — but a question with an edge to it. The KJV sharpens the picture: "Why standest thou afar off, O LORD? why hidest thou thyself in times of trouble?" God is pictured as a figure who has stepped back and away — present somewhere, but distant, and deliberately out of sight just when the help is needed most.

The psalm never apologizes for asking. This is what makes the Psalter a true prayer book: it gives words not only to the mountaintop but to the moment the sky goes quiet. The complaint is allowed to be a prayer. And notice the question is never "Are you there?" — the wicked says that (v.4); the psalmist never does. He says, "Why so far?" Distance, not absence. The whole poem is the slow closing of that distance.

📖
The load-bearing words

Four Words Under the Complaint

Psalm 10 is a courtroom of opposites — one figure swollen and loud, the others bent low and silent. A handful of Hebrew words name the two sides, and the English softens them.

רָשָׁע
rasha
the wicked · the guilty one

The word that dominates the psalm, ringing out again and again. Rasha is courtroom language — not just a "bad person" but the one declared guilty, in the wrong, condemned. He is the single villain in the singular here, a portrait so vivid he becomes every oppressor at once: loud, lifted up, sure he'll never answer for it.

עָנִי
ani / anaw
the afflicted · bowed-down · humble

The "poor" the wicked hunts. Ani isn't only the financially poor — it's the bent-over one, pressed down by force, with no power to push back. Its cousin anaw (v.12, 17) carries the same posture turned Godward: the humble who have nowhere to look but up. The whole psalm is told from their eye-level — close to the ground.

יָתוֹם
yatom
the fatherless · the orphan

The fatherless child — in the ancient world, the most defenseless person imaginable, with no one to stand in the gate and plead the case. Yatom appears twice (v.14, 18), and where it appears, God appears as the one who steps in. In Israel's law the orphan is God's own special concern; the psalm ends by making Him the orphan's defense attorney.

דָּכָה
dakah
to be crushed · broken small

Verse 10's verb for what happens to the helpless: dakah means crushed, beaten down, broken into pieces. It's the same root behind "a broken and a contrite heart" elsewhere in the Psalms. Here it's literal violence — the strong collapsing the weak under their weight. The psalm refuses to look away from it.

Psalm 10:2–11

A Long Look at the Arrogant

Now the psalm does something unusual. Instead of rushing to the rescue, it holds the camera on the oppressor for ten verses — letting us see him whole. He is proud (v.2), greedy (v.3), boastful, and convinced he is untouchable: "I will not be moved… through all generations I will never be in trouble" (v.6).

"He keeps himself in a secret place like a lion in his hole, waiting to put his hands on the poor man, and pulling him into his net."
Psalm 10:9 · BBE

The imagery turns predatory. The Hebrew verb arab — to lie in ambush — runs through these verses: he crouches in the dark places of the villages (v.8), eyes "secretly set against the poor," then springs. The psalm stacks two pictures that don't normally go together: a lion (raw power) and a hunter's net (cold calculation). The oppressor is both — savage and strategic, patient and merciless. He doesn't lose his temper; he lurks.

But the engine under all of it is a single sentence he keeps muttering to himself. Three times the psalm reports what the wicked "says in his heart" (v.6, 11, 13) — and the middle one is the darkest:

אֵין אֱלֹהִים
ein Elohim
"there is no God" · "God is not in his thoughts" — v.4

This is the hinge of the whole psalm. The KJV renders verse 4, "God is not in all his thoughts," and the BBE, "All his thoughts are, There is no God." Scholars call this practical atheism — not a philosopher denying that God exists, but a man living as though God does not see, count, or care. He doesn't argue God away; he simply leaves no room for Him in a single calculation. And so he says it out loud in verse 11: "God hath forgotten: he hideth his face; he will never see it." The wicked's entire confidence rests on one lie — that the LORD is not watching.

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Teaches from the Hebrew under the English, and ein Elohim is a phrase he'd open like a hinge. It's the same confession as the "fool" who "says in his heart, There is no God" in Psalm 14 — and the Hebrew nabal, "fool," means not a low IQ but a moral collapse, a heart that has shoved God out of the room. The wicked's pride and his atheism are the same act: if no one is watching, I am the highest thing there is. Every cruelty in the psalm grows from that one whispered lie.

The answer the whole psalm was reaching for

"The LORD Is King Forever"

Featured Verse · Psalm 10:16
יְהוָה מֶלֶךְ עוֹלָם וָעֶד
YHWH melekh olam va'ed — "The LORD is King for ever and ever"
"The Lord is King for ever and ever; the nations are gone from his land."
Psalm 10:16 · BBE

Here is the answer to verse 1. The psalm opened with a God who seemed to stand far off; it lands on a God who has been enthroned the entire time. Two Hebrew words turn the whole poem on its hinge.

מֶלֶךְ
melekh
king · reigning sovereign

The wicked spent ten verses acting as if he were king — "I will not be moved," answerable to no one. Verse 16 quietly overrules him: YHWH melekh. There is already a throne, and it is already occupied. The oppressor's whole empire was built on a vacancy that was never vacant.

עוֹלָם וָעֶד
olam va'ed
forever and ever · age upon age

Olam stretches past the horizon of time; va'ed piles "and beyond" on top of it. The wicked boasted he'd be safe "through all generations" (v.6) — a hollow forever. This is the true forever, set right beside his counterfeit. His reign is a rumor; God's is the only one that outlasts the threshing floor.

The man who said "there is no God" is answered not with an argument but with a coronation. The LORD did not stand far off because He was absent. He stood as King — and the closing verses prove He was watching every crushed and fatherless face the whole way down.

Psalm 10:12–14

"Arise, O LORD"

Between the long stare at the wicked and the closing confidence comes the turn — the moment the prayer stops describing and starts pleading. It opens with one of the great verbs of the Psalter:

"Up! O Lord; let your hand be lifted: give thought to the poor."
Psalm 10:12 · BBE

The KJV reads "Arise, O LORD; O God, lift up thine hand." It's a summons borrowed from Israel's wilderness march: whenever the ark set out, Moses cried, "Rise up, LORD" (Numbers 10:35). The psalmist is calling the God who seemed seated and far off to stand and move. And then, in verse 14, the complaint quietly answers itself:

"You have seen it; for your eyes are on sorrow and grief… the poor man puts his faith in you; you have been the helper of the child who has no father."
Psalm 10:14 · BBE

This is the exact reversal of the wicked's lie. He said, "God will never see it" (v.11); the psalmist answers, "You have seen it." The verb that the oppressor denied is the very thing now affirmed, in God's face. The hidden God of verse 1 turns out to be the seeing God of verse 14 — His eyes were never closed; they were fixed on exactly the sorrow the wicked assumed went unnoticed.

Where this psalm reaches across Scripture

One Portrait, Quoted and Echoed

Psalm 10's picture of the godless oppressor didn't stay in the Psalter. It became one of the Bible's standard portraits of the human heart without God — and Paul reaches for it directly.

Romans 3 weaves this psalm into its great indictment. When Paul stacks up Old Testament verses to prove that "all have sinned," he pulls a line straight from here:

"Whose mouth is full of cursing and bitterness…" — quoting the oppressor "full of cursing and deceit" of Psalm 10:7.
Romans 3:14 · drawing on Psalm 10:7

Paul's whole chain in Romans 3:10–18 is built from Psalms (14, 5, 140, 10, 36) and Isaiah, and it climaxes on the diagnosis Psalm 10 already made: "There is no fear of God before their eyes" (Romans 3:18). That is ein Elohim in the New Testament — the heart with no room for God, now named as the root of the whole human predicament.

The fatherless thread runs the length of the Bible. God's claim on the orphan in verses 14 and 18 isn't new in the psalm — it's the law speaking: "You are not to be hard on any widow, or child whose father is dead… and my wrath will be burning" (Exodus 22:22–24). And it reaches all the way to the New Testament's plainest definition of true religion: "to take care of children who have no fathers… in their trouble" (James 1:27). The God who judges for the fatherless (Psalm 10:18) asks His people to mirror exactly that.

CM
Chuck Missler

Loved tracing how the New Testament quotes the Old, and he'd flag what Paul is doing in Romans 3: building a legal brief. Each citation is an exhibit, and Psalm 10 supplies the witness to the mouth "full of cursing." He'd point out the design across the two halves: Psalm 9 closes asking that the nations "know themselves to be but men," and Psalm 10 closes on the same word — "that the man of the earth may no more oppress" (v.18). The acrostic that began in praise ends by putting frail, dust-made man back in his place under a King who reigns forever.

Where the long stare ends

Closing Reflection

Psalm 10 travels the whole arc of a hard prayer — from "Why so far off?" to "You have seen it" to "The LORD is King forever" — and it hands the reader three things to carry out of the dark:

The question is allowed

The psalm makes no apology for asking "Why do you hide?" Honest lament is not the opposite of faith — it's faith addressing God instead of giving up on Him. The complaint itself is a prayer.

He was never not watching

The wicked's whole power rested on one lie — "God will never see it." Verse 14 overturns it: "You have seen it." The distance of verse 1 was never blindness. His eyes were on the sorrow the whole time.

The throne is occupied

Every cruelty in the psalm assumed a vacant heaven. The last verses answer: the LORD is King forever, the defender of the crushed and the fatherless. The seeming silence was a King already on the throne.

The psalm that began with a God who stood far off ends with a God who sits enthroned forever — and who bends His ear to "the prayer of the poor" (v.17). The arrogant built an empire on a vacancy. Psalm 10's last word is that the vacancy was never empty.

Next · Psalm 11

"In the LORD I put my trust"

Psalm 10 ended on the throne. Psalm 11 opens being told to flee from it — "Flee as a bird to your mountain," the voices say, "the foundations are destroyed." David answers the panic with the same conviction Psalm 10 reached: "The LORD is in his holy temple, the LORD's throne is in heaven." The King who was watching the oppressor now watches the trembling — and His eyes still test the children of men.
