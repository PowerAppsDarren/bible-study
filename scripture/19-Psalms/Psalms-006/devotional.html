Psalm 6 · Where it sits

The First of the Seven

Psalm 1 set two ways at the door; Psalm 2 raised the King on Zion. Now, with Psalm 6, the Psalter does something new — it breaks down and weeps. This is the first of the book's many laments, the raw prayers of someone in trouble who has nowhere left to turn but upward.

And the church gave it a particular seat. Since at least the time of Augustine and Cassiodorus, Psalm 6 has been counted as the first of the seven Penitential Psalms — 6, 32, 38, 51, 102, 130, and 143 — the prayers the ancient church read in seasons of repentance and on Ash Wednesday. It opens a series that runs all the way to Psalm 51, David's great confession. If you want to learn how the Bible cries, you start here.

The Hebrew superscription reads: "To the chief musician; with stringed instruments; al ha-sheminith; a psalm of David." That phrase sheminith means "upon the eighth." Scholars take it two ways — either a musical setting (a tune, or playing on an eight-stringed instrument), or a lower octave / bass register, a deep voice fitting for a song this heavy. Both readings agree it is a performance note; the exact mechanics are lost to us.

BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Reads each psalm by its genre first, and he'd name this one immediately: an individual lament, the most common type in the Psalter. The form has a shape you can almost diagram — an opening cry, a description of distress, a plea for rescue, a reason God should act, and then a turn to trust. Psalm 6 runs the whole pattern in ten short verses. Knowing the genre tells you to expect the hinge before you reach it.

⚖️
Psalm 6:1

"Do Not Rebuke Me in Anger"

"O Lord, do not be bitter with me in your wrath; do not send punishment on me in the heat of your passion."
Psalm 6:1 · BBE

Notice what the prayer does not ask. It doesn't say "do not rebuke me" or "do not correct me" — it says do not do it in anger, in the heat of wrath. The two verbs underneath are yakach, to reprove or set right, and yasar, to discipline or instruct (the WEB keeps both: "rebuke… discipline"). The psalmist isn't pleading to dodge correction. He's pleading that it not come as the fire of judgment.

It's the same instinct a child has who knows the difference between a parent's firm hand and a parent's fury. Discipline he can survive; the heat of wrath he cannot. So the very first words of the Bible's first lament draw a line between being corrected and being consumed — and throw the whole weight of the prayer onto God's mercy rather than the sufferer's record.

JB
John Bevere

Teaches the difference between God's correction and God's wrath, and verse 1 is built on exactly that seam. The psalmist doesn't argue that he deserves nothing — he simply runs toward the God whose anger he fears, not away. That is the paradox at the heart of a penitential psalm: the only refuge from God is God. Reverence and rescue ask from the same lips.

The load-bearing words

Four Words from the Sickbed

Psalm 6 is the cry of someone physically and emotionally undone — "wasted away," bones shaking, soul in anguish. A handful of Hebrew words carry that collapse, and they're far more bodily than the English suggests.

חָנַן
chanan
be gracious · show favor · have mercy

"Have mercy on me" in v.2 is chaneni, from chanan — to bend down in kindness to someone who has no claim on you. It pictures a superior stooping to an inferior with undeserved favor. The psalmist asks for nothing he is owed; he throws himself entirely on God's free generosity.

רָפָא
rapha
heal · mend · restore

"Make me well" (v.2) is rephaeni, from rapha — the ordinary verb for a physician healing a wound or mending what is broken. It is the same root behind Yahweh-Rapha, "the LORD who heals you" (Exodus 15:26). The trouble is not abstract: something in him is broken, and he asks the Healer to set it.

בָּהַל
bahal
be terrified · shake · be dismayed

The word that frames the whole psalm. His bones are troubled (v.2), his soul is troubled (v.3) — both bahal, a violent trembling, terror that shakes you to the marrow. Then at the very end the same verb turns: his enemies will be "deeply troubled" (v.10). The terror that fills him in verse 2 lands on his foes by verse 10.

נֶפֶשׁ
nephesh
soul · throat · whole living self

"My soul" (vv.3–4) is nephesh — never a ghost trapped in a body. It means the whole living person, breath and appetite and life itself (its root sense is "throat," the place breath passes). When he begs God to "make my soul free," he is asking for his entire self to be rescued from death's grip.

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Opens the Hebrew under the English, and bahal is the kind of thread he'd pull. The same trembling word stitches the psalm shut: the terror that shakes his bones in verse 2 is the terror that overtakes his enemies in verse 10. Nothing in his circumstances has changed by the last line — but the dread has moved off of him. The prayer didn't lift the storm; it moved him out of its path.

Psalm 6:5

"In Death There Is No Memory of You"

"For in death there is no memory of you; in the underworld who will give you praise?"
Psalm 6:5 · BBE

This is one of the most honest verses in the Old Testament, and it deserves to be read without softening. The "underworld" is Sheol — the shadowy realm of the dead in Hebrew thought. And the psalmist makes a blunt argument: save me, because corpses don't praise You. In Sheol there is no memory, no thanksgiving, no worship rising to God.

שְׁאוֹל
she'ol
the grave · the realm of the dead

Read it for what it is. Most of the Old Testament has only a dim and shadowy hope about life beyond the grave; the full light of resurrection comes later in Scripture. So the psalmist's reasoning is raw and almost commercial: I am no use to You dead. The same argument recurs across the Psalter — "What profit is there in my blood, when I go down to the pit? Shall the dust praise You?" (Psalm 30:9), and "the dead don't praise the LORD" (Psalm 115:17). Hezekiah prays it nearly word for word on his sickbed (Isaiah 38:18).

It is not the last word Scripture speaks about death — but it is an honest word, and the Bible lets it stand. The sufferer here isn't reciting a creed about the afterlife; he's a man who can feel the grave's cold breath and is bargaining for one more day to worship in the land of the living.

🌊
Psalm 6:6–7

A Bed Swimming in Tears

"All the night I make my bed wet with weeping; it is watered by the drops flowing from my eyes. My eyes are wasting away with trouble…"
Psalm 6:6–7 · BBE

Here the lament reaches its lowest point, and the imagery turns to water. The Hebrew is almost extravagant — the older translations catch it: "all the night make I my bed to swim; I water my couch with my tears" (KJV). He pictures the bed floating, the couch dissolving, a flood pouring from his eyes until they themselves "waste away" and "grow old" from grief.

It is hyperbole, of course — and that is exactly the point. The psalm gives sorrow permission to be loud. It does not tidy the grief into something dignified; it lets the man drown the room. The Bible's first lament teaches that real prayer can sound like this — sleepless, exhausted, weeping all night — and still be heard. Verse 7 is the bottom of the well. The very next line, everything changes.

The verse the whole psalm turns on

The Sudden Turn

Featured Verse · Psalm 6:8
סוּרוּ מִמֶּנִּי כָּל־פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן
suru mimmeni kol-po'alei aven — "Depart from me, all you workers of evil"
"Go from me, all you workers of evil; for the Lord has given ear to the voice of my weeping. The Lord has given ear to my request; the Lord has let my prayer come before him."
Psalm 6:8–9 · BBE

One line ago the man was drowning his bed. Now, with no transition at all, the voice stands up. The weeping becomes a command: get away from me, you who do evil. What happened between verse 7 and verse 8?

He became sure he was heard. Three times in two verses the same news rings out — the Lord has heard… the Lord has heard… the Lord has received my prayer. Notice the psalm never reports that the sickness lifted or the enemies left. The circumstances are unchanged. What changed is a settled confidence that the prayer landed — and that certainty is enough to turn a sob into a stand.

This is the hinge every lament swings on. The same tears God "gave ear to" are the proof of the turn — He did not wait for the weeping to stop before He heard it. The prayer was answered while it was still wet.

Psalm 6, woven into the Gospels

Words the Son Would Borrow

This little sickbed prayer turns up, in startling places, on the lips of Jesus — once in His own agony, once on the day of judgment. Two of its phrases are stitched straight into the Gospels.

The troubled soul — taken up in Gethsemane's shadow. Psalm 6:3 cries, "My soul is in bitter trouble." In the Greek Old Testament that line reads he psyche mou etarachthe. Stand it beside Jesus at the edge of the cross:

"Now is my soul troubled; and what am I to say? Father, keep me from this hour."
John 12:27 · BBE

The same verb, the same trouble of soul. The Gospels show Jesus carrying the language of the Psalter's laments into His own suffering — praying the prayers the psalmists first wept.

"Depart from me" — returned at the last day. Psalm 6:8 commands, "Depart from me, all you workers of iniquity." In the Sermon on the Mount Jesus speaks an almost identical sentence — but now He is the Judge:

"And then will I say to them openly, I never had knowledge of you: go from me, you workers of evil."
Matthew 7:23 · BBE
CM
Chuck Missler

Loved tracing a phrase across the whole library, and he'd flag this echo. In Psalm 6 a rescued sufferer tells evildoers to depart, because his prayer was heard. In Matthew 7 the Judge tells evildoers to depart, because He never knew them. The same four words, two scenes — and the difference between them is the whole question of being known by God. The Old Testament's words keep coming back wearing new authority.

Where the first lament leaves us

Closing Reflection

Psalm 6 is the Bible's first weeping, and it hands every reader who has ever lain awake in trouble three things to carry:

Grief gets a voice

The psalm doesn't tidy the sorrow — it lets the bed swim and the eyes waste away. The first lament gives raw, sleepless, exhausting grief full permission to be spoken aloud to God.

Mercy, not record

From verse 1 the whole weight lands on God's grace, not the sufferer's standing — chanan, the bending-down of undeserved kindness. The plea is to be corrected, never consumed.

Heard while still wet

Nothing in his circumstances changed at the hinge — but the certainty that God listened turned a sob into a stand. The prayer was answered before the tears were dry.

Ten verses run the full arc of a lament — from the heat of feared wrath, through a flooded bed and the cold edge of the grave, to a man rising off the floor because the Lord has given ear to the voice of his weeping. The Psalter has learned to cry. And it has learned, in the same breath, that the crying is heard.

Next · Psalm 7

"O Lord my God, I put my faith in you"

From a sickbed of tears to a courtroom of pursuit. Psalm 7 — a shiggaion of David — turns from weeping to a plea for justice, as the falsely accused runs to God as Judge and asks Him to weigh the heart. The lament becomes an appeal.
