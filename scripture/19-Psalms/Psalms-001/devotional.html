Psalm 1 · The Preface

The Gate of the Psalter

One hundred and fifty psalms follow — laments, hymns, royal songs, raw complaints, towering praise. But the first one wears no title. No "of David," no tune, no occasion. While most psalms carry a superscription, Psalm 1 stands bare at the threshold like a doorpost. Whoever gave the Psalter its final shape set this wisdom poem first on purpose: before you pray a single line, you are handed a map with two roads on it.

And it isn't standing there alone. Psalm 1 (the two ways) and Psalm 2 (the LORD's anointed King) are widely read as a twin gateway — both untitled, set as a matched pair. The very first word of Psalm 1 is ashrei, "blessed"; the very last line of Psalm 2 is ashrei again — a bracket that frames the two poems as one front door. Some early manuscripts even count them as a single psalm.

CM
Chuck Missler

Loved noticing structure like this — the ashrei at the start of Psalm 1 answered by the ashrei at the close of Psalm 2, an envelope binding the pair. He'd point out that the New Testament reads them this way too: in Acts 13:33, Paul quotes Psalm 2, and a number of early manuscripts label it "the first psalm." Two doors, one gateway — wisdom on one leaf, the King on the other.

The Psalter is built in five books, each closing in a burst of praise — a deliberate echo of the five books of Moses. Psalm 1 sets the tone for all five: the whole songbook opens by sending you to the Torah.

🚶
Psalm 1:1

The Slow Slide of Verse One

"Happy is the man who does not go in the company of sinners, or take his place in the way of evil-doers, or in the seat of those who do not give honour to the Lord."
Psalm 1:1 · BBE

Read it slowly and you'll feel the floor tilting downward. The Hebrew lines up three verbs in descending order — walk, stand, sit (the WEB keeps the staircase: "walks… stands… sits"). And three companies that get worse as you go: the counsel of the wicked, the path of sinners, the seat of scoffers.

It's a portrait of how a life drifts. First you walk — you stroll alongside, you listen to the advice. Then you stand — you stop and settle into the road they're on. Finally you sit — you pull up a chair and make it your home, in the seat of the scoffers, the ones who mock what's holy. From a passing influence to a fixed address. The blessed life, the psalm says, is the one that never lets that first stroll become a seat.

📖
The load-bearing words

Four Words at the Threshold

Psalm 1 is short — six verses — but a handful of Hebrew words carry the whole weight. English smooths them; the originals are sharper and more physical.

אַשְׁרֵי
ashrei
O the blessednesses of…

The first word of the Psalter. It's a plural construct — literally "O the happinesses of the man!" — a glad exclamation, not a pronouncement. It is not the usual word for blessing, barukh (which flows down from God). Ashrei describes the person from below: look how well-set his life is. The Greek Old Testament renders it makarios — the very word that opens the Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3).

תּוֹרָה
torah
instruction · teaching · direction

Usually flattened to "law," but the root yarah means to throw, to shoot, to point the way — like aiming an arrow at a target. Torah is less a rulebook than a direction for living, God's teaching that aims a life true. The blessed man's whole delight (v.2) is set there.

הָגָה
hagah
to murmur · mutter · meditate

"Meditate" in v.2 isn't silent and empty-minded. Hagah is a low, muttered sound — the same verb used of a lion growling over its prey (Isaiah 31:4) and a dove moaning (Isaiah 38:14). To meditate on Torah is to chew it under your breath, turning the words over aloud day and night until they're part of you.

דֶּרֶךְ
derek
way · road · path

The master-image of the whole psalm. A derek is a worn road, the direction a life travels. It opens the psalm (the "way of sinners," v.1) and closes it (the "way of the upright" set against the "way of the sinner," v.6). Everything in between is a study of two roads going opposite places.

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Teaches from the Hebrew under the English, and hagah is the kind of word he'd open up: biblical meditation is the opposite of emptying the mind. Where some traditions empty, the Hebrew fills — a quiet, constant muttering of God's words, the way you'd hum a song you can't stop. You become what you murmur over day and night.

The verse the whole psalm grows from

"Like a Tree Planted by the Water"

Featured Verse · Psalm 1:3
עֵץ שָׁתוּל עַל־פַּלְגֵי מָיִם
ets shatul al-palgei mayim — a tree transplanted beside channels of water
"He will be like a tree planted by the rivers of water, which gives its fruit at the right time, whose leaves will ever be green; and he will do well in all his undertakings."
Psalm 1:3 · BBE

Two small Hebrew words make this picture stronger than the English lets on.

שָׁתוּל
shatul
transplanted · set in place

Not a wild tree that happened to sprout near water — shatul means deliberately transplanted, lifted and set in place by another hand. The blessed man doesn't stumble onto the water-source. He is planted there, on purpose, by Someone.

פַּלְגֵי מָיִם
palgei mayim
channels of water

Not a wild river but irrigation channels — dug, divided, directed streams that never run dry in drought. The tree's roots reach a steady supply that doesn't depend on the weather. Fruit in season, leaf that never withers — a life that stays green when everything around it browns.

This is the engine of the whole psalm. The man rooted in Torah (v.2) becomes the tree by the water (v.3) — fed, fruitful, evergreen. The metaphor isn't decoration; it's a promise about where life actually comes from.

💨
Psalm 1:4–5

The Tree and the Chaff

Now the psalm pivots hard. Three short Hebrew words in verse 4 slam the door on the alternative: lo-ken ha-resha'im — "Not so the wicked." Whatever the tree is, they are the exact opposite.

🌳💧
the righteous — a rooted tree
🌾💨
the wicked — chaff on the wind
"The evil-doers are not so; but are like the dust from the grain, which the wind takes away."
Psalm 1:4 · BBE

The Hebrew word is motz — chaff, the dry husk that comes off the grain on the threshing floor. Picture the winnowing: the farmer tosses the crop into the evening wind. The heavy kernels fall back to be gathered; the light, empty husks blow away and are gone. The wicked have no weight, no root, no water — nothing to hold them when the wind comes. The tree is fixed; the chaff has no fixed place at all.

"For this cause there will be no mercy for sinners when they are judged, and the evil-doers will have no place among the upright."
Psalm 1:5 · BBE

The same image returns at the harvest of the age. John the Baptist preached it almost word-for-word: the Coming One "will gather his wheat into the barn, but the chaff he will burn" (Matthew 3:12). Wheat or chaff, tree or husk — Psalm 1 has been sorting the threshing floor from the very first verse.

🌿
The tree, replanted across Scripture

One Image, Whole-Bible Roots

Psalm 1's tree isn't a one-off. The same picture grows in several places, and tracing it shows how deliberately the psalm is woven into the rest of Scripture.

Jeremiah 17 says it almost identically. The prophet sets the same two ways side by side — and lands on the same tree:

"…he will be like a tree planted by the waters, that spreads out its roots by the river… its leaf will be green; and will not be troubled in the year of drought."
Jeremiah 17:8 · paraphrase of WEB

Jeremiah adds the secret of the roots: the tree flourishes because "he has put his hope in the Lord" (17:7). The water Psalm 1 points to is, finally, trust in God Himself.

Psalm 92 carries it into old age. The righteous, "planted in the house of the Lord," still bear fruit when they're gray — "they will be full of sap and green" (Psalm 92:14). And Revelation lands the image at the very end of the Bible: the tree of life beside the river of the water of life, its leaves "for the healing of the nations" (Revelation 22:2). The tree planted by the water in the Psalter's first poem is still standing — by living water — in Scripture's last chapter.

BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Reads the Bible as one unified literary work, genre by genre, and he'd flag what kind of poem this is: Psalm 1 is wisdom literature, cut from the same cloth as Proverbs — the genre of the two ways. Setting a wisdom poem as the doorway tells you how to read everything inside: not as magic words, but as a path to walk. Jesus preaches the same fork in Matthew 7:13–14 — the narrow road and the broad — the two ways of Psalm 1 with a gate added.

🛣️
Psalm 1:6

The Way That Is Known

"Because the Lord sees the way of the upright, but the end of the sinner is destruction."
Psalm 1:6 · BBE

The psalm ends where it began — on the word derek, the way — with the two roads finally named side by side. But look at the verb the BBE renders "sees." The Hebrew is yada: to know — and in Hebrew, to know is not to observe from a distance but to be intimately acquainted with, to care for, to attend to.

So the two ways don't merely end differently. The deeper contrast is this: one road is known by God — watched over, walked with, kept — and the other simply perishes, fading out like chaff with no one holding it. The whole psalm comes to rest on that asymmetry. The righteous are not just headed somewhere better; they are known the whole way there.

🕊️
Where the gate opens

Closing Reflection

Psalm 1 is the doorway, and it hands every reader the same three things to carry into the 149 psalms that follow:

Be planted

The tree doesn't find the water; it's transplanted to it. A fruitful, evergreen life isn't self-grown — it's a life set down on purpose beside a steady source, and rooted there.

Mind the slide

Walk, stand, sit — verse one maps how a life drifts from a passing influence to a fixed address. The blessed road guards the first step, before the stroll becomes a seat.

The way is known

The two roads don't just end differently. One is known by God — watched over the whole length of it — while the other blows away like chaff. To be on it is to be kept.

Two ways diverge at the door of the Psalter — the tree and the chaff, the rooted and the rootless, the known and the lost. Everything after this poem is sung by someone who has already chosen which road they're on. The whole songbook begins by pointing you to the water.

Next · Psalm 2

"Why do the nations rage?"

The gateway has two doors. Psalm 1 showed the two ways; Psalm 2 raises the King. From the blessed man rooted by the water to the LORD's Anointed enthroned on Zion while the nations storm — and it closes on the same word it opened with: ashrei, "blessed are all who take refuge in Him." The other half of the door swings open.
