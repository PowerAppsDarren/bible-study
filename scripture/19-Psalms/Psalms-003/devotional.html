Psalm 3 · The Title Line

The First Psalm With a Story

Psalms 1 and 2 stood at the door of the songbook untitled — bare wisdom and the enthroned King. Now, with Psalm 3, the Psalter does something new: it gives a song a setting. Above the first line sits a superscription — in the Hebrew it is counted as verse 1 — that names the day this prayer was forged.

"A Psalm of David. When he fled from Absalom his son."
Psalm 3 · Superscription

The story is told in 2 Samuel 15–17. Absalom, David's own son, steals the hearts of Israel and seizes the throne. David flees Jerusalem on foot, climbing the Mount of Olives "weeping, with his head covered and his feet bare" (2 Samuel 15:30). A man named Shimei pelts him with stones and curses; a trusted counselor, Ahithophel, defects. By any reckoning the king is finished. This is the night Psalm 3 comes from — the morning prayer of a man whose own house has turned against him.

Of the roughly thirteen psalms whose titles tie them to a moment in David's life, this is the first the reader meets. The superscription is not decoration: it turns an abstract lament into a real dawn, and lets every later sufferer borrow a king's own words for their worst morning.

BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Reads the Bible as one unified story, and he'd set this psalm back into its narrative. Psalm 3 is a lament — the most common type of psalm — but here it comes welded to a chapter of 2 Samuel, so you can stand where David stood: on the Mount of Olives, looking back at a lost city, with the Jordan still ahead. The geography is real, the betrayal is real, and that's what makes the trust in the second half cost something.

Psalm 3:1–2

The Cruelest Taunt

"Lord, how greatly are they increased who make attacks on me! in great numbers they come against me. Unnumbered are those who say of my soul, There is no help for him in God."
Psalm 3:1–2 · BBE

The psalm opens not with the enemy's swords but with their arithmetic. Three times in two verses David counts them — increased… in great numbers… unnumbered. The Hebrew leans on a single root, rabab/rav, "to be many," hammering it like a man surrounded counting the campfires on the ridge.

But the deepest wound is in verse 2, and it isn't a weapon — it's a sentence: "There is no help for him in God." The enemies aren't merely hunting David; they have written his obituary and signed God's name to it. The taunt is theological. It says the one thing a fleeing believer most fears in the dark: that heaven itself has closed the case. And right there, on that exact word, the Psalter sets down its very first Selah.

The Psalter's first pause

What Is "Selah"?

Seventy-one times across thirty-nine psalms a single mysterious word interrupts the song: Selah. Psalm 3 is where the reader first meets it — and it lands three times here (after verses 2, 4, and 8), marking the seams of the whole poem. Yet for all its frequency, no one is certain what it means. Honesty about that uncertainty is part of reading it well.

סֶלָה
selah
a musical or liturgical marker · meaning uncertain

Scholars hold several views, none decisive. Many tie it to the root salal, "to lift up," and read it as a direction to the musicians — raise the music here, a swell or instrumental interlude. Others hear a liturgical pause: stop, breathe, and weigh what was just said. The ancient Greek translators rendered it diapsalma ("a musical break"); some later Jewish tradition glossed it as "forever." What every view shares is a break in the flow — a built-in rest stop in the song.

Whatever the precise mechanics, notice where the breaks fall. The first Selah comes right after the despairing taunt — "there is no help for him in God" — as if the psalm forces you to sit in the worst sentence before it answers it. The pause is placed at the point of greatest pain.

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Works from the Hebrew under the English, and he'd resist the urge to over-define Selah where the text leaves it open. The honest reading is that it's a marker of pause and weight — a place the music or the soul is told to stop and dwell. However the ancient choir performed it, the effect on the page is the same: the song refuses to rush past the hard line. You are made to linger before you are allowed to hope.

📖
The load-bearing words

Two Words That Turn the Tide

Verse 3 swings the whole psalm around with one Hebrew word — but — and then hands David two images for who God is in the siege. Both are sharper in the Hebrew than the English lets on.

מָגֵן
magen
shield · encircling cover

From ganan, "to cover, surround, defend." A magen was the small round shield a soldier strapped to his arm — but David says God is a shield "round me", on every side, not just the front. It is the same word God speaks to Abram: "I am your shield" (Genesis 15:1), the first time the LORD names Himself a defense. A fugitive with no walls left is told he is already walled in.

מֵרִים רֹאשִׁי
merim roshi
the lifter of my head

David fled the city with his head covered in shame (2 Samuel 15:30); now he calls God the one who lifts the head. The phrase elsewhere means to restore an honor that was lost — Pharaoh "lifts up the head" of a prisoner to give back his office (Genesis 40:13). The bowed, hunted king says the very posture of his disgrace is in God's hands to reverse.

Set the two verses side by side and you can watch the poem turn from the count of enemies to the cover of God:

⚔️🗣️
the foes — many, and writing him off
🛡️🙌
the Lord — a shield, a glory, a lifted head

The verse the whole psalm turns on

"But You, O Lord, Are a Shield Around Me"

Featured Verse · Psalm 3:3
וְאַתָּה יְהוָה מָגֵן בַּעֲדִי
ve-attah YHWH magen ba'adi — but you, O LORD, are a shield about me
"But your strength, O Lord, is round me, you are my glory and the lifter up of my head."
Psalm 3:3 · BBE

Everything before this verse counts the enemy; everything after it rests in God. The hinge is the small word ve-attah — "but you." Over against the unnumbered foes and their verdict stands one second-person pronoun: You. The whole lament pivots on turning from the size of the threat to the face of God.

And the three names David reaches for are exactly the three things the crisis stripped from him. He has lost his safety — God is his shield. He has lost his throne and honor — God is his glory. He flees with his head bowed in shame — God is the lifter of his head. The verse doesn't deny the loss; it answers each loss with God Himself.

This is the engine of the psalm, and the reason it became the prayer of every cornered believer after David. The math of the enemy never changes in these eight verses — they are still many at the end. What changes is that the sufferer turns from counting them to naming his shield.

Psalm 3:4–6

He Lay Down and Slept

Between the shield (v.3) and the sleep (v.5) sits a quiet line: "I send up a cry to the Lord with my voice, and he gives me an answer from his holy hill" (v.4). The "holy hill" is Zion — the very city David just fled. Cut off from the ark and the sanctuary, he discovers that the answer reaches him anyway, out in the wilderness. Then comes the verse that has steadied the frightened for three thousand years:

"I took my rest in sleep, and then again I was awake; for the Lord was my support."
Psalm 3:5 · BBE

Read it against the night it describes. An army is hunting him. His son wants him dead. A betrayed king, sleeping rough beyond the Jordan, would be forgiven for lying awake straining at every sound. Instead David's testimony is almost scandalously simple: I lay down. I slept. I woke up. The proof that God was his support is not that the enemy vanished — it's that he rested while they remained. Sleep, here, is an act of faith.

"I will have no fear, though ten thousand have come round me, putting themselves against me."
Psalm 3:6 · BBE

Now the arithmetic of verse 1 returns — but it has lost its power. The "great numbers" have become a named figure, ten thousand, and David sets one word against the whole host: "I will have no fear." The same encircling that terrified him in verse 1 ("come against me… round me") is met in verse 3 by a God who is also "round me." The siege is answered by a better encirclement.

🌿
One night-prayer, read across the canon

"I Lay Down and Slept"

That single line — lie down, sleep, wake — sends roots through the rest of Scripture. Following them shows how deliberately this morning-prayer is woven into the larger story.

The companion psalm answers it at dusk. Psalm 3 is a morning psalm; Psalm 4, its twin, is the evening prayer — and it ends on the same note of fearless sleep:

"I will take my rest on my bed in peace… for you, Lord, only, make my resting-place safe."
Psalm 4:8 · BBE

Together the two psalms bookend a whole day — I slept and woke in the morning, I will lie down in peace at night — both resting on the same God who keeps a hunted man.

The early church heard more than a nap. From at least Augustine onward, Christian readers heard in "I lay down and slept; I woke again" a figure of death and resurrection — one who lies down in the grave and rises because God sustains him. Whether or not David intended that depth, the language gave the church a vocabulary: the believer's sleep, like the believer's death, is the kind you wake from.

And the shield runs the length of the Bible. The magen of verse 3 reaches back to God's first self-naming — "I am your shield, your very great reward" (Genesis 15:1) — and forward into the armor of the New Testament, where faith itself becomes "the shield… to put out the fire-darts of the Evil One" (Ephesians 6:16). David's encircling shield is the same protection, named again for every later believer.

CM
Chuck Missler

Loved tracing a single image from end to end of the canon, and he'd flag the pattern here: a betrayed king crosses the Kidron, climbs the Mount of Olives in tears, and is handed over by a trusted friend — and then prays through the night with confidence that he will rise. He'd note that another Son of David would one night cross the same brook, climb the same hill, and be betrayed by a friend (John 18:1). Old patterns, he'd say, are rehearsals. Readers across traditions hear the echo differently; the geography, at least, is the same.

Psalm 3:7–8

"Arise, O Lord"

"Come to me, Lord; keep me safe, O my God; for you have given all my haters blows on their face-bones; the teeth of the evil-doers have been broken by you."
Psalm 3:7 · BBE

After the rest comes the request — and the word David chooses is loaded with the memory of Israel's marches. The BBE's "Come to me" renders the Hebrew qumah, "Arise!"

קוּמָה
qumah
arise · rise up

This is the ancient war-cry over the ark. Whenever it set out, Moses cried, "Rise up, O Lord, and let your haters be put to flight" (Numbers 10:35). David — cut off from that very ark in Jerusalem — reaches for its battle-prayer in the wild. He has no ark, but he still has the God who answers "Arise."

יְשׁוּעָה
yeshuah
salvation · deliverance · rescue

From yasha, "to save, to bring into a wide open space" — the root behind the names Joshua and Yeshua. The psalm of a king whose own army has shrunk ends by giving the credit away: deliverance is not a tactic or a throne, it belongs to the LORD. The fleeing man's last word is not about his odds but about God's.

The blows are pictured bluntly — enemies struck "on the cheek-bone," their "teeth broken." It is the image of a predator defanged: the threat is left alive but disarmed, its bite removed. And then the psalm lifts off its own crisis entirely:

"Salvation comes from the Lord; your blessing is on your people."
Psalm 3:8 · BBE

The last line widens past David altogether. The prayer began with one man counting his enemies; it ends with a blessing on "your people" — the whole flock of God. A private nightmare on the run becomes a public confession: rescue is the LORD's to give, and His blessing rests not just on the king but on everyone who belongs to Him. The Psalter's first storyboarded lament closes with its third and final Selah — the long rest after the battle.

selah
the final pause — rest after the deliverance (v.8)

What the morning prayer leaves you

Closing Reflection

Psalm 3 walks the full arc of a bad morning — lament, trust, confidence, petition — with a Selah set at each seam. It hands the cornered reader three things to carry into the dark:

Count the shield, not the foes

The enemies never shrink across these eight verses — they are "many" at the start and "ten thousand" near the end. What changes is that the sufferer stops counting them and names his shield. The turn is the small word but you.

Sleep is an act of faith

A hunted king's proof that God sustained him is not a vanished army but a good night's rest. To lie down and sleep while the threat remains is itself a confession that Someone else is keeping watch.

Rescue belongs to the Lord

The prayer that began with one man's odds ends giving the deliverance away — salvation is the LORD's — and widens to a blessing on all His people. Your worst morning, prayed honestly, opens onto everyone's hope.

A betrayed king climbed a hill in tears, lay down in the open with an army behind him, and woke to write that God was a shield around him. The Psalter's first lament is also its first proof that the dawn comes — and that the one who lies down trusting is the one who wakes.

Next · Psalm 4

"You will let me take my rest in peace"

If Psalm 3 is the morning prayer of a hunted man, Psalm 4 is its evening twin — the same fearless sleep, now sung at nightfall. From "I slept and woke; the Lord was my support" to "I will lie down in peace, for you, Lord, make my resting-place safe." One day, bookended by trust, closes as the sun goes down.
