Psalm 9 + Psalm 10 · One Poem

The Broken Acrostic

Before a single word of meaning, look at the shape. Psalm 9 is built as an acrostic — successive stanzas begin with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet, aleph, bet, gimel… marching in order. But the alphabet runs out of room in Psalm 9. It reaches only about the midpoint, around the letter kaph, and then Psalm 10 picks up the rest — lamed through taw — finishing the song the ninth psalm started.

א  ב  ג  ד  ה  ו  ז  ח  ט  י  כ │ ל  מ  נ  ס  ע  פ  צ  ק  ר  שׁ  ת
Psalm 9 (aleph → kaph)  │  Psalm 10 (lamed → taw) — one alphabet, split across two psalms

This is why the ancient versions count them differently. In the Hebrew Bible they are two psalms; but in the Greek Septuagint (LXX) and the Latin Vulgate — the Bibles of the early church — Psalms 9 and 10 are numbered as a single psalm. (That one-psalm count is what shifts the Greek and Latin numbering of most of the Psalter by one.) Several Hebrew manuscripts also run them together, and Psalm 10 carries no superscription of its own — unusual in this stretch of the Psalter, as if it were never meant to stand alone.

The two halves do two different jobs. Psalm 9 is the praise half: God the righteous Judge has already ruled the nations, and the singer gives thanks. Psalm 10 is the lament half: the wicked still seem to get away with it, and the singer cries "Why do you stand far off, O Lord?" Held together, they tell the truth about justice — already, and not yet.

BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Reads the Bible as one unified literary work, and he'd start with form before content: an acrostic is a memory-craft, an A-to-Z built so the whole subject is covered — "everything from aleph to taw." That a poem about God's justice is shaped this way is itself a claim: the Judge's case against evil is complete, ordered, alphabet-thorough. And a broken acrostic, split across a praise psalm and a lament, quietly admits the order isn't all visible yet.

The superscription

"Upon Muth-labben"

The psalm's heading reads, in the older translations, "To the chief Musician upon Muth-labben, A Psalm of David" (Psalm 9, title, KJV). The phrase Muth-labben — Hebrew al-muth labben — has puzzled readers for two thousand years.

Read literally it could mean something like "upon the death of the son" (muth, "death"; ben, "son"), and some have hunted for an occasion to match. But the heading sits where psalm titles usually name a tune or a musical setting — the way other psalms say "upon Lilies" or "according to The Dove." Most scholars take it as exactly that: the name of a melody now lost, an instruction to the choir-master that meant something obvious in the temple and means nothing certain to us. It is an honest reminder that these were living songs with music we no longer have — and that not every detail of the text was preserved with its meaning intact.

Psalm 9:4–8

The Courtroom of Heaven

The praise here is not vague gratitude — it is verdict-shaped. The singer thanks God the way a vindicated litigant thanks the judge who finally heard the case:

"For you gave approval to my right and my cause; you were seated in your high place judging in righteousness."
Psalm 9:4 · BBE

Every key word in this psalm comes from the language of the law court. God is not a distant force; He is seated — enthroned — and the throne is specifically a judgment seat (v.7). From it He issues true verdicts not just for one person but for the whole world.

שָׁפַט · מִשְׁפָּט
shaphat · mishpat
to judge · justice / verdict

The drumbeat of the psalm. Shaphat is the verb "to judge, to govern, to set right"; mishpat is the noun — the verdict, the just decision, the rule of right. In Hebrew, to "judge" is not only to condemn; it is to put things in order, to rule a people justly. God "prepared his throne for mishpat" (v.7) and "will judge (shaphat) the world in righteousness" (v.8).

גּוֹיִם
goyim
the nations · the peoples

The word rings through the psalm — the goyim, the nations (older Bibles, "the heathen"). Psalm 9 is not a private quarrel; its courtroom is global. God "has rebuked the nations" (v.5), "will judge the world" (v.8), and ends with the prayer "let the nations be judged before you… let the nations know that they are only men" (vv.19–20). The arrogant peoples are weighed and found to be merely mortal.

"But the Lord is King for ever: he has made ready his high seat for judging. And he will be the judge of the world in righteousness, giving true decisions for the peoples."
Psalm 9:7–8 · BBE

Set against the nations whose very name God "blots out forever" (v.5), this is the great contrast of the psalm: their memory perishes; His throne endures. Empires rise and their cities fall to ruin (v.6), but the LORD remains, seated, ready to judge. The thrones that look permanent are the ones that vanish; the throne that looks unseen is the one that lasts.

The verse the whole psalm rests on

"The LORD Sits Enthroned Forever"

Featured Verse · Psalm 9:7
וַיהוָה לְעוֹלָם יֵשֵׁב
va-YHWH le'olam yeshev — "but the LORD sits / abides forever"
"But the Lord is King for ever: he has made ready his high seat for judging."
Psalm 9:7 · BBE

One ordinary Hebrew verb makes this verse the hinge of the psalm — and ties the whole poem together.

יָשַׁב
yashav
to sit · dwell · be enthroned

Yashav simply means to sit, to remain, to stay put. It is the same verb used in verse 4 — God "sat (yashav) judging in righteousness" — and again here: "the LORD sits (yashav) forever." To sit, in a royal setting, is to reign; a king who sits is a king who rules. The added word le'olam — "forever" — turns an ordinary posture into the deepest comfort of the psalm: the Judge never leaves the bench.

כִּסֵּא
kisse
throne · seat of judgment

The "high seat" God "has made ready" is the kisse — the throne. Crucially, He prepares it for mishpat, "for judgment." This is not a throne of mere splendor but a working bench. The same image runs through Scripture to its end, where the One on the throne declares, "Behold, I make all things new" (Revelation 21:5). The seat readied in Psalm 9 is still occupied.

Everything the psalm hopes for hangs on this one line. Because the LORD sits — settled, enthroned, permanent — the nations' fury is temporary and the sufferer's wait is safe. The throne is the reason the song can begin with praise before the trouble is over.

Psalm 9:9–10, 18

A High Tower for the Crushed

Here the great public courtroom narrows to a single bruised person. The same God who judges empires bends to the one being trampled — and the psalm gives Him a name drawn from the architecture of safety.

מִשְׂגָּב
misgav
high refuge · unreachable stronghold

BBE's "high tower," KJV's "refuge." A misgav is a height too high to be stormed — a crag, a fortress set where no attacker can climb. The root means "to be inaccessibly high." To call God a misgav is to say the oppressed are lifted to a place the enemy simply cannot reach.

דַּךְ
dak
crushed · bruised · beaten down

The people God shelters are the dakkim — the "crushed down" (BBE), the ones ground low by stronger hands. The word pictures something pulverized, pressed flat. The stunning pairing is the point: the highest stronghold is given to the most crushed — the loftiest refuge for the lowest people.

"The Lord will be a high tower for those who are crushed down, a high tower in times of trouble; and those who have knowledge of your name will put their faith in you."
Psalm 9:9–10 · BBE

And the psalm makes the crushed a promise about memory. The danger for the powerless is always that they will be forgotten — that no one is keeping the record. Psalm 9 says Someone is:

"For the poor will not be without help; the hopes of those in need will not be crushed for ever."
Psalm 9:18 · BBE

"He is not without thought for the cry of the poor" (v.12); the needy "shall not always be forgotten" (v.18, WEB). The God who blots out the name of the arrogant nations (v.5) keeps the names of the afflicted. Two ledgers, kept in exact opposite ways.

Psalm 9:15–16

The Pit They Dug

How does the Judge act? Often, the psalm says, He simply lets evil complete itself. The trap closes on the trapper; the hunter is caught in his own net.

"The nations have gone down into the hole which they made: in their secret net is their foot taken."
Psalm 9:15 · BBE

This is one of the Bible's recurring pictures of justice — evil is self-defeating, the snare springs back on the one who set it. And it is no accident here: just two psalms earlier David sang the same image. "He made a hole deep in the earth, and is falling into the hole which he made; the trouble he made comes back on himself" (Psalm 7:15–16, paraphrase, BBE). Psalm 9 takes that private proverb and makes it the verdict on the nations.

"The Lord has given knowledge of himself through his judging: the evil-doer is taken in the net which his hands had made. (Higgaion. Selah.)"
Psalm 9:16 · BBE

Notice what the verdict reveals: "the Lord has given knowledge of himself through his judging" (KJV: "the Lord is known by the judgment which he executeth"). God's justice is not only how He acts — it is how He introduces Himself. And the verse ends with two of the Psalter's most mysterious words:

הִגָּיוֹן סֶלָה
higgaion selah
a musical pause · "let the strings sound"

Higgaion comes from hagah — "to murmur, mutter, meditate" (the same root behind "meditate" in Psalm 1:2). Here it seems to mark a musical interlude — a low resounding of the strings, a beat to let the thought sink in. Selah, scattered through the Psalms, is itself uncertain: most take it as a liturgical pause — stop, lift the music, weigh what was just sung. Together: hold here. Let the justice of God resound and settle.

The placement is deliberate. The poem has just said the wicked fall into their own pit and that God is known by such judgments — and then it stops the music and tells you to sit with it.

The justice thread, traced

One Theme, Many Strands

Psalm 9 is a knot where several of Scripture's cords meet. Tracing them shows how tightly the psalm is woven into the books around it.

The pit that backfires runs everywhere. Beyond Psalm 7:15, the same image surfaces in Proverbs — "whoever digs a pit will fall into it" (Proverbs 26:27) — and in the story of Haman, hanged on the gallows he built for Mordecai (Esther 7:10). Psalm 9 turns a wisdom proverb into a hymn.

The nations weighed and found mortal. The closing prayer — "let the nations know that they are only men" (v.20) — anticipates the long biblical theme of God judging the proud peoples, gathered up at last in the throne-scenes of Revelation, where "the nations were angry" and the One on the throne judges the earth (Revelation 11:18).

"For the needy shall not always be forgotten, nor the hope of the poor perish forever."
Psalm 9:18 · WEB

The unforgotten poor. This single line opens onto a whole stream — the God who "remembers" the afflicted (v.12) is the God of Hannah's song, of Mary's Magnificat ("he has put down the mighty… and lifted up the humble," Luke 1:52), of every passage where the lowly are remembered and the proud are scattered. Psalm 9 is one of the headwaters.

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Teaches from the Hebrew under the English, and he'd linger on a quiet detail: the same God who blots out the name of the arrogant (v.5) is the God who cannot forget the name of the afflicted (v.18). The very word for what happens to the proud — to be forgotten, erased from memory — is exactly what God refuses to let happen to the crushed. What you do to the powerless writes your own entry in His book.

Where the song leaves you

Closing Reflection

Psalm 9 sings thanks from inside a trial that isn't over — and it hands the reader three things to carry into the lament that follows in Psalm 10:

The throne is occupied

Empires fall and their cities go to ruin, but the LORD sits — enthroned, forever, on a seat readied for judgment. The Judge never leaves the bench, and that is why praise can come before the rescue does.

Two ledgers are kept

The God who blots out the name of the proud is the same God who keeps the name of the crushed. The needy "shall not always be forgotten" — Someone is keeping the record no one else bothers to keep.

The alphabet is unfinished

The acrostic stops halfway on purpose. Psalm 9's "already" of justice waits for Psalm 10's "not yet" to be answered. The song knows the order isn't all visible — and sings anyway.

This is a poem for the gap between what God has done and what we still wait to see Him do. It dares to begin "I will give you praise, O Lord, with all my heart" (v.1) while the haters are still circling — because it has looked up and seen the throne that does not move.

Next · Psalm 10

"Why do you keep far off, O Lord?"

The alphabet finishes here. If Psalm 9 is the praise half — the Judge enthroned, the nations weighed — Psalm 10 is the lament half of the same broken acrostic: the wicked who boasts, hunts the poor, and says in his heart "God has forgotten." Same poem, other side. The thanksgiving turns and asks the hardest question — and the second half of the song begins.
