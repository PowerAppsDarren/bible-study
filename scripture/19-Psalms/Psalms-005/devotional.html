Psalm 5 · The Superscription

A Song for the Wind Instruments

Psalm 5 carries a heading the BBE leaves out but the older versions keep: "To the chief Musician upon Nehiloth, A Psalm of David." Nehiloth is a musical direction whose root is widely tied to the word for a flute or pipe — so this is a psalm set, most likely, for wind instruments. From its first breath, Psalm 5 is meant to be played, not just read: a melody for the reed pipes at the start of the day.

And it belongs to a thread running through the early Psalter. Psalm 3 is an evening psalm — "I took my rest in sleep, and then again I was awake" (3:5). Psalm 4 hands the day over at night — "I will take my rest in peace" (4:8). Then Psalm 5 turns to the morning. Read together they frame an ordinary day in prayer: lie down, rise, and the first act of the new light is to speak to God before anyone else.

BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Reads the Bible as a unified literary work, attentive to how a book is arranged — and he'd point to the way these opening psalms are sequenced like the hours of a day. Psalm 3 at night, Psalm 4 lying down, Psalm 5 at dawn: the editors of the Psalter weren't filing songs at random. They set them so the reader prays through the clock, and the morning psalm comes loaded with the imagery of the morning sacrifice and the opening of the temple gates.

🌅
Psalm 5:1–3

Three Ways to Be Heard

"Give ear to my words, O Lord; give thought to my heart-searchings. Let the voice of my cry come to you, my King and my God; for to you will I make my prayer."
Psalm 5:1–2 · BBE

The prayer opens by asking to be heard three different ways, because there are three kinds of prayer here. First the words — the things actually said. Then the heart-searchings — the part with no words at all. Then the voice of my cry — the loud, urgent shout. Spoken, unspoken, and unable-to-be-spoken: the psalmist asks God to catch every register of him at once.

"My voice will come to you in the morning, O Lord; in the morning will I send my prayer to you, and keep watch."
Psalm 5:3 · BBE

Twice in one verse: in the morning… in the morning. The repetition is the point. This is not a panic prayer flung up in a crisis but a standing appointment — the first thing, every day. And it ends on a posture the older versions render "I will look up" or "watch expectantly": having laid the prayer out, the psalmist keeps watch like a sentry on the wall, scanning the horizon for the answer to come.

📖
The load-bearing words

Three Words at First Light

Three Hebrew words in the first three verses carry the whole posture of the psalm. English softens them; the originals are far more physical — a groan, an altar, and a watchman.

הָגִיג
hagig
meditation · murmuring · groaning

The BBE's "heart-searchings" (v.1) is the noun hagig, from the same root hagah that anchors Psalm 1's "meditate." It's not silent reflection — it's a low, inarticulate murmur, the sound a person makes when the heart is too full for sentences. The psalmist asks God to read the groan beneath the words, the prayer that never makes it into speech.

עָרַךְ
arak
to lay out · arrange · set in order

"I will send my prayer" (v.3) flattens a vivid verb. Arak is what a priest does when he lays the wood and the pieces of the offering in order on the altar (Leviticus 1:7–8), and what a soldier does when he draws up a battle line. The morning prayer is arranged — set out deliberately, like a sacrifice — and then left for the fire of God to fall.

צָפָה
tsaphah
to watch · keep lookout · scan

"And keep watch" (v.3) is tsaphah — the verb for a watchman posted on the city wall, straining toward the horizon for the first sign of a messenger. Prayer here isn't a coin dropped and forgotten. Having laid the offering out, the psalmist stands his post and watches, expecting an answer to crest the hill.

PS
Perry Stone

Works from Hebrew roots and Israel's worship calendar, and arak is exactly the kind of word he'd light up: the morning sacrifice (the tamid) was laid on the altar at dawn, the first act of the temple day. When David says he will "lay out" his prayer in the morning, he's casting his words as that morning offering — prayer arranged on the altar like the wood and the lamb, sent up with the smoke at first light.

Psalm 5:4–6

The God Who Cannot Host Evil

The middle of the psalm shifts from the temple to the courtroom. Before God grants the prayer, the psalmist establishes who this Judge is — and the description is built almost entirely on what God will not tolerate in His presence.

"For you are not a God who takes pleasure in wrongdoing; there is no evil with you. The sons of pride have no place before you; you are a hater of all workers of evil."
Psalm 5:4–5 · BBE

The logic is the logic of a guest list. Evil "may not dwell with" God (as the WEB puts it) — it has no standing to lodge in His house. The arrogant cannot take a position in His sight. This is the hard hinge of the psalm: the same God who welcomes the praying heart at dawn is a God in whose presence cruelty simply has no room to stay.

"You will send destruction on those whose words are false; the cruel man and the man of deceit are hated by the Lord."
Psalm 5:6 · BBE

Notice what the verses keep circling: the bloodthirsty and the deceitful — violence and lies. These are the two sins the psalm names again and again, and they are the two it will return to when it describes the enemy's mouth. The Judge's character in verses 4–6 is the exact measure the accuser will be weighed against in verse 9.

🛐
Psalm 5:7–8

"But as for me, I will come into your house"

Against everyone who has no place before God comes the great reversal of the psalm — two words in Hebrew, wa-ani, "but as for me." The wicked cannot enter; the psalmist will. And he is careful to say why: not on his own merit, but on God's mercy.

"But as for me, I will come into your house, in the full measure of your mercy; and in your fear I will give worship, turning my eyes to your holy Temple."
Psalm 5:7 · BBE

The entrance is gated by two words. He comes in the abundance of God's chesed — God's covenant mercy, the only credential that opens the door — and he bows in yirah, the fear of the Lord, the awe that knows whose house this is. Welcome and reverence held together: he is glad to be admitted and trembling to be there at once.

"Be my guide, O Lord, in the ways of your righteousness, because of those who are against me; make your way straight before my face."
Psalm 5:8 · BBE
דֶּרֶךְ
derek — way, road, the path a life travels
make level the road before my face

The prayer of verse 8 is for a road. Derek is the Psalter's master-image — the same word that opened and closed Psalm 1's "two ways" — and here the psalmist asks God to make His way straight, level, clear ahead of him, "because of those who watch for me to slip." Surrounded by enemies who want him to stumble, he asks not for their removal but for sure footing: a road he can walk without falling.

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Teaches from the Hebrew under the English, and he'd hold up derek beside Psalm 1. The Psalter's first poem set the two ways — the road of the righteous that God knows, and the road of the wicked that perishes — and Psalm 5 is a man praying his way onto the right road: "make your derek straight before my face." The same image that opened the book is now a personal request: level the path, and keep my feet on it.

The verse the New Testament reaches back for

"Their Throat Is an Open Grave"

Featured Verse · Psalm 5:9
קֶבֶר־פָּתוּחַ גְּרוֹנָם
qever-patuach gronam — their throat is an opened grave
"For no faith may be put in their words; their inner part is nothing but evil; their throat is like an open place for the dead; smooth are the words of their tongues."
Psalm 5:9 · BBE

This is the most-quoted line in the psalm, and the image is brutal. The enemy's deceit is traced from the inside out — the inward part, the throat, the tongue — and at the center sits a single horrifying picture: an open grave.

קֶבֶר פָּתוּחַ
qever patuach
an open grave · a gaping tomb

A grave left uncovered — the stench of death and decay rising out of it. The flattering throat looks like a mouth, but what comes out of it is the smell of the tomb. Smooth words on the surface; rot underneath. Charm in service of death.

חָלַק
chalaq
smooth · slippery · flattering

"Smooth are the words of their tongues" — chalaq means slick, frictionless, slippery. It's the polished flattery that gives you nothing to hold and leaves you sliding. The same root names the slippery path the wicked are sent down in Psalm 35:6. The smoother the words, the more dangerous the footing.

And this is the verse Paul reaches back for. In Romans 3:13, building his case that "all have sinned," he stitches together a chain of Old Testament lines on the corruption of human speech — and he pulls Psalm 5:9 in word for word: "Their throat is an open grave… the poison of asps is under their lips." The accuser David faced becomes, in Paul's hands, a portrait of the whole race apart from grace.

CM
Chuck Missler

Loved tracing how the New Testament quotes the Old, and Romans 3:10–18 is a classic example he'd unpack: Paul builds a catena — a deliberate chain of citations — and Psalm 5:9 is one of its links, set beside Psalms 14, 140, 10, and Isaiah 59. One man's enemies in Psalm 5 become Paul's diagnosis of all humanity. The open grave was never just about David's accusers; it was a mirror.

Psalm 5:10–12

Two Crowds, Two Endings

Like Psalm 1 before it, Psalm 5 closes by sorting the world into two crowds and tracing each to its end. First the deceivers, handed over to the weight of their own schemes:

"Send them to destruction, O Lord; let their evil designs be the cause of their fall… because they have gone against your authority."
Psalm 5:10 · BBE

Note the shape of the judgment: the psalmist doesn't ask to strike them himself. He asks God to let them fall by their own counsels — to be tripped by the very traps they set. And the charge isn't private grievance: "they have gone against your authority" — they have rebelled against God, not merely against David. Then the psalm pivots, for the last time, to the other crowd:

🪦💨
the deceiver — fallen by his own designs
🛡️🎉
the trusting — surrounded with favor
"But let all those who put their faith in you be glad with cries of joy at all times… For you, Lord, will send a blessing on the upright man; your grace will be round him, and you will be his strength."
Psalm 5:11–12 · BBE

The psalm that began in a groan ends in a shout of joy. And the last image is the one that answers every threat in it — the BBE's "your grace will be round him" is, in the Hebrew, a shield.

צִנָּה
tsinnah
the great body-shield

Not the small round buckler (magen) strapped to the arm — tsinnah is the full-length body shield, tall enough to crouch behind, the one a shield-bearer carried to cover the whole soldier (it's the shield in Goliath's armor, 1 Samuel 17:7). Here the psalmist is covered head to foot.

רָצוֹן
ratson
favor · good pleasure · delight

What forms the shield is ratson — God's favor, His glad goodwill. Verse 12 says God will "crown" or surround the righteous with favor as with a body-shield (ka-tsinnah ratson ta'tarennu). The same favor wraps all the way around — not a wall in front, but a circle. Favor on every side.

It is the perfect close. The accuser's throat was an open grave — exposed, gaping, leaking death. The righteous are the opposite: closed in on every side by the favor of God, like a soldier crouched safe behind the great shield while the arrows fall.

🕊️
Where the morning leads

Closing Reflection

Psalm 5 walks a full arc — from the first murmur before dawn to a shout of joy under God's shield — and it leaves three things in the reader's hands:

Lay it out early

The morning prayer is arak — arranged like wood on the altar, set out in order, then watched over like a sentry on the wall. Prayer here is the day's first deliberate act, not its last resort.

In by mercy, not merit

"But as for me, I will come into your house" — and the door opens only on the abundance of God's mercy, entered in the fear of the Lord. Welcome and reverence are held together at the threshold.

Surrounded by favor

The open grave gapes; the favored are closed in. The psalm ends with the great body-shield — God's goodwill drawn all the way around the one who trusts Him, covering every side at once.

Two crowds, two mouths, two endings: the throat that is an open grave, and the life that is surrounded as with a shield. Psalm 5 is the prayer of someone who has chosen which one he wants to be — and who gets up before the day to ask for it. The morning watch begins by laying the heart on the altar and looking up.

Next · Psalm 6

"O Lord, do not be bitter with me"

From the morning watch to the long night of tears. Psalm 6 is the first of the seven Penitential Psalms — a prayer drenched in weeping, "all the night I make my bed wet with weeping" — where the sentry who watched for the dawn now waits out the dark, and learns that God hears the sound of crying too.
