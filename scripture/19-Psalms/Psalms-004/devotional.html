Psalm 4 · The Heading

To the Chief Musician, on Strings

Most English Bibles print a line above verse 1 that is easy to skip: "To the chief Musician on Neginoth, A Psalm of David." In the Hebrew Bible that line is verse 1 — it isn't an editor's caption but part of the inspired text. And here it does something it has never done before in the Psalter: it hands the song to a music director and names the instruments.

The Hebrew lam'natseach ("to the chief musician" / "for the choir leader") opens fifty-five psalms, but Psalm 4 is the first of them. Bin'ginot means "with stringed instruments" — from nagan, to pluck a string. So before a single word of prayer, the heading tells you this is no private murmur: it is a composed piece, scored for the lyre and the harp, handed to the worship leader to be sung by the gathered people.

Psalm 3 — the morning prayer
🌙
Psalm 4 — the evening prayer

Psalms 3 and 4 are widely read as a matched pair. Psalm 3:5 says, "I lay down and slept; I woke again, for the LORD sustained me" — a waking song. Psalm 4:8 answers it at the other end of the day: "In peace I will both lie down and sleep." One greets the dawn after a guarded night; the other lies down trusting the night to come. Read together, they bookend a single day in God's keeping.

BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Reads the Bible as a unified literary work, attentive to how a book is arranged. He'd point to the pairing of these two psalms — morning and evening, waking and lying down — as a deliberate piece of editing, not a coincidence. The superscriptions are part of the literature: the moment Psalm 4 is handed "to the choirmaster, with strings," a personal lament becomes congregational song, written to be sung by everyone, not just overheard.

Psalm 4:1

The Shape of a Settled Heart

Psalm 4 moves in four clean beats, and the movement is the message — it travels from agitation to rest. It opens with a petition to God (v.1), turns to rebuke the crowd who scorn the psalmist (v.2–3), issues an invitation to trust instead of rage (v.4–5), and lands in settled peace (v.6–8). The prayer ends asleep.

"Give answer to my cry, O God of my righteousness; make me free from my troubles; have mercy on me, and give ear to my prayer."
Psalm 4:1 · BBE

The opening line carries a memory inside it. The KJV catches it: "thou hast enlarged me when I was in distress." The Hebrew picture is spatial — God has taken a person hemmed into a tight, narrow place and given them room to breathe. The psalmist asks for fresh mercy on the strength of mercy already shown. The prayer is anchored not in panic but in a track record.

📖
The load-bearing words

Four Words That Carry the Psalm

Eight short verses, but a few Hebrew words do the heavy lifting. English smooths them; the originals are sharper — and two of them are knit together by a shared root.

חָסִיד
chasid
the godly · loyal · set-apart one

In v.3 the BBE softens it ("his mercy for me"), but the Hebrew says God "has set apart the chasid for himself." A chasid is a person marked by chesed — covenant loyalty and steadfast love. The claim is bold: the one mocked by the crowd is the very one God has reserved as His own.

חֶסֶד
chesed
steadfast love · covenant mercy

The root under chasid. Chesed is the great Old Testament word for God's faithful, committed love — mercy that keeps its promises. The godly one is not self-made; he is made godly by the love that claims him. Loyalty flows down from God before it is ever lived back up to Him.

בָּטַח
batach
to trust · lean the full weight on

The hinge of v.5: "put your trust in the LORD." Batach is not cautious hoping — its sense is to fling yourself down, secure, on something solid, to lean your whole weight on it. The cure the psalm prescribes for a raging heart is not to win the argument but to transfer the weight onto God.

סֶלָה
selah
pause · lift up · (musical rest)

It appears twice (v.2, v.4) — and Psalm 4 is the first psalm to use it after Psalm 3. Its meaning is uncertain; most link it to a musical or liturgical pause, perhaps "lift up" the voices or instruments. Either way it asks the singer to stop and let the line settle before going on. The agitation of v.2 needs a held breath.

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Teaches from the Hebrew under the English, and the chesed / chasid link is exactly the kind of thread he'd pull. The word for "godly one" is built from the word for God's own loyal love — so a chasid is, at root, a person of God's love, someone the steadfast love of God has taken hold of. You don't earn the name by your devotion; you wear it because His chesed reached you first.

Psalm 4:2–4

Tremble — and Be Still

"O you sons of men, how long will you go on turning my glory into shame? how long will you give your love to foolish things, going after what is false?"
Psalm 4:2 · BBE

Now the prayer turns outward to the crowd dragging the psalmist's name through the mud. The double "how long…" is the ache of someone slandered and worn down. But the answer he gives them is startling. Instead of cursing them, he hands them a path out:

"Let there be fear in your hearts, and do no sin; have bitter feelings on your bed, but make no sound."
Psalm 4:4 · BBE

The Hebrew opens with rigzu — "tremble," "quake," "be deeply moved." Whatever stirs you, the psalm says, take it to your bed — the place of honest, silent reckoning — and do not let the feeling drive you into sin. Feel it fully; act on it wrongly, never. Then: "be still." The same instruction the whole psalm is built to teach.

CM
Chuck Missler

Loved tracing a verse from the Hebrew through the Greek into the New Testament — and v.4 is a classic case. The ancient Greek Old Testament (the Septuagint) rendered rigzu as orgizesthe, "be angry." That is the exact wording Paul quotes in Ephesians 4:26 — "Be angry, and do not sin; do not let the sun go down on your wrath." Paul even keeps the nightfall setting: sort it out before bed. The Hebrew "tremble" and the Greek "be angry" both land in the same place — strong feeling, surrendered before sleep.

The verse the whole psalm lies down on

"In Peace I Will Lie Down and Sleep"

Featured Verse · Psalm 4:8
בְּשָׁלוֹם יַחְדָּו אֶשְׁכְּבָה וְאִישָׁן
b'shalom yachdav eshkevah v'ishan — in peace, all at once, I will lie down and sleep
"I will take my rest on my bed in peace, because you only, Lord, keep me safe."
Psalm 4:8 · BBE

The whole prayer has been moving toward this bed. The agitation of verse 2, the trembling of verse 4, the doubting crowd of verse 6 — all of it resolves into a body laying itself down. Two Hebrew words make the rest deeper than the English lets on.

שָׁלוֹם
shalom
peace · wholeness · all is well

Shalom is not merely the absence of conflict — though the conflict of v.2 is real. It is wholeness, soundness, everything in its right place. The psalmist lies down not because the slander has stopped, but because something larger than the slander holds him. Peace is a Person before it is a feeling.

לְבָדָד
l'vadad
alone · only · in solitude

The line says God "only" (l'vadad) makes me dwell in safety — but the word also means "alone," "in solitude." Read either way the comfort holds: God alone is the reason, and even sleeping alone, the psalmist is not unguarded. The one who could not silence his enemies can still close his eyes.

Sleep is the most defenseless thing a person does. To lie down is to admit you are not in control of the night — and to do it in peace is the purest evidence of trust there is. The psalm doesn't end with the enemies defeated; it ends with the psalmist asleep — which, in its own way, is the deeper victory.

Psalm 4:6

Lift Up the Light of Your Face

"There are numbers who say, Who will do us any good? the light of his face has gone from us."
Psalm 4:6 · BBE

The crowd's despair is timeless: "Who will show us any good?" They are scanning the horizon for any sign of better days. The psalmist answers them not with a plan or a harvest, but with a face. The KJV and ASV catch the prayer underneath: "Lord, lift thou up the light of thy countenance upon us."

That phrase is not new. It reaches back to the oldest blessing in Scripture — the words Aaron and his sons were commanded to speak over Israel:

"May the Lord let his face be shining on you, and have mercy on you… may the Lord's approval be resting on you and may he give you peace."
Numbers 6:25–26 · BBE

The psalmist takes the priestly blessing — the shining face, the lifted countenance, ending in shalom — and turns it into a prayer. When the many ask "who will show us good?", his answer is the Aaronic benediction itself: let God's face shine, and the peace of verse 8 is already on its way. The blessing that ends in peace becomes the prayer that ends the psalm in sleep.

PS
Perry Stone

Draws out the Hebrew-roots and liturgical patterns behind a text — and he'd note that Psalm 4 deliberately echoes the Aaronic blessing of Numbers 6, the words spoken over Israel in the temple to this day. The "light of the face" is Hebrew shorthand for God's favor turned toward you — the opposite of a hidden face. The psalmist isn't inventing a phrase; he's reaching for the most familiar blessing in Israel and praying it back to God in the dark.

Psalm 4:7

More Than Grain and Wine

"Lord, you have put joy in my heart, more than they have when their grain and their wine are increased."
Psalm 4:7 · BBE

This is the psalm's quiet turn. The crowd of verse 6 measures "good" by the harvest — grain and wine, the two great signs of a bumper year, the festival markers of plenty. The psalmist sets his joy beside theirs and finds it heavier. A gladness already given, before any harvest comes in, and not dependent on it.

The contrast is the whole point. Their joy waits on the weather and the threshing floor; his is already in his heart. One is borrowed from the storehouse and can fail; the other is given by the God of verse 1, and cannot. It is the gladness of verse 7 that makes the sleep of verse 8 possible — a settled heart lies down easily.

Where the prayer comes to rest

Closing Reflection

Psalm 4 is the Psalter's first evening hymn, and it carries a whole day's worth of trust into the dark. Three things travel with it to the bed:

Tremble, then be still

The psalm doesn't deny the anger or the ache — it tells you to feel it on your bed, then hand it over before sleep. Strong feeling, surrendered, never driven into sin. The same word Paul carries into Ephesians 4:26.

You are set apart

The crowd may scorn the godly one, but God has reserved the chasid for Himself. The name isn't earned by devotion; it's worn because His steadfast love took hold first.

Lie down in peace

The prayer ends asleep. To lie down defenseless and rest in shalom is the purest proof of trust there is — peace that rests on a Person, not on the harvest or the silenced enemy.

A song handed to the choirmaster, scored for strings, sung as the lamps go out. It begins hemmed into a tight place and ends with room to breathe; it begins with a doubting crowd and ends with a sleeping saint. Psalm 4 teaches the whole congregation how to put a day down — and close their eyes in the keeping of the only One who keeps them safe.

Next · Psalm 5

"Give ear to my words, O Lord"

If Psalm 4 lies down at dusk, Psalm 5 rises with the sun: "In the morning you will give ear to my voice." Where Psalm 4 ends in peaceful sleep, Psalm 5 wakes to lay a morning prayer before God and watch — another song handed to the choirmaster, this time scored for the flutes. The evening prayer gives way to the watch of the dawn.
