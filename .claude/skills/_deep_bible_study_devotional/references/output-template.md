## Section types and when to use each

### 🔥 Setting the stage
Use as the opening section in almost every devotional. Establishes:
- What the prophet/author was facing
- Who the original audience was
- The cultural and historical context
- The chapter's thrust in one sentence

### 💔 The grieving heart
Use when the chapter shows God's sorrow over His people, not just His anger. Especially good for:
- Prophetic indictments where the tone is broken-heartedness
- Chapters that reveal God's father-heart
- Lamentations of any kind

### 🩹 The wounded body
Use when the text describes systemic corruption, sickness, or collapse imagery (Isaiah 1:5-6 style). Especially when the metaphor is medical or anatomical.

### ⚠️ The hard pivot
Use when the chapter shifts dramatically — from glory to judgment, from invitation to indictment, from praise to lament. Mark the pivot point and explain why.

### 🎭 The performance / deception
Use when the chapter exposes hypocrisy, vanity, or cultural performance. Connect to modern celebrity culture, social media, religious theater.

### 🌍 Modern application
Use when there's a clear bridge to the present cultural moment. Bridge categories:
- Political dysfunction → Isaiah 3
- Reality TV / performance → wherever vanity is exposed
- Education / indoctrination → wherever truth is suppressed
- Entertainment industry → wherever spiritual dimension is removed
- Economic anxiety → wherever idols of wealth appear

### 👁️ The spiritual layer
Use when the chapter has implications for the unseen reality. Pull back the curtain on the spiritual war.

### 🐍 The enemy's strategy
Use when the chapter exposes a pattern that fits the adversary's broader playbook. Reference the Eden directives framework (see `eden-directives-framework.md`).

### ♟️ The strategic parallel
Use when you can map God's directive vs Satan's counterfeit cleanly. Often as a table.

### 🏔️ The mountain / kingdom
Use when the chapter has governmental or kingdom imagery. Mountains in Hebrew prophecy almost always mean kingdoms.

### 🌿 The remnant / restoration
Use when the chapter pivots toward hope, even briefly. The remnant theme runs through the whole Bible — name it where it surfaces.

### ☁️ The cloud and fire
Use when divine presence imagery appears. Connect to the Exodus pattern.

### ❄️ The verse that hit you
Always use this section (or equivalent striking emoji like 🌅, 💎, 🔥). This is the chapter's heart for this reader. Treat it like the climactic teaching.

### 🕊️ Closing reflection
Always include. Pulls threads, lands the truth, points forward.

## Hebrew/Greek word study format

Inline format (preferred):
> The Hebrew word for "nourished and brought up" — גִּדַּלְתִּי (giddalti) — is the word used for a parent tenderly raising a child. God is not speaking as a king betrayed by subjects. He is speaking as a father whose children won't come home for dinner anymore.

Block format (for the featured verse section):
> "Come now, and let us reason together"
> The Hebrew is וְנִוָּכְחָה (niwwakhah) — it means to argue the case, to contend, to be proven right together. God is not commanding. He is inviting.

Always include:
1. The original word in script
2. Transliteration in parentheses
3. Why it matters here
4. Connection to where else it appears in Scripture (if relevant)

## Cross-reference patterns that work

- "This is the New Testament echo of [OT passage]..."
- "Paul writes the same thing in [NT reference]..."
- "Jesus quotes this almost word-for-word in [reference]..."
- "Revelation [chapter] is John watching this happen in real time..."
- "The thread connects to..."
- "Watch what happens when you put this next to..."

## The bridge from ancient to modern

Don't just list parallels. Show the mechanism. Example that worked:

> Isaiah is making a direct callback: the breath that animates every human being — that you feel right now in your chest — is on loan. It was placed there by Someone else. It can be recalled.

The shift from ancient text to "right now in your chest" is the move that lands. Every devotional should have at least one of these.

## What the closing reflection does

Three jobs:
1. **Compress the chapter into one sentence** that the reader can carry
2. **Name the believer's position today** in light of what was taught
3. **Point forward** to Christ, the restoration, the hope

Example that worked:
> Isaiah 3 says: when a nation removes God, God removes its wisdom — and what's left are children playing king, a culture obsessed with its own reflection, and a people who can't tell the difference between entertainment and governance.
>
> Isaiah 4 says: but there will be a remnant. And the Branch will come. And the cloud and fire will return. And everything that was defiled will be washed. And God will cover His people like He always has.
>
> The world looks like Isaiah 3 right now.
>
> But we are living toward Isaiah 4.
>
> Hold on to that.

Notice: compression, present-tense believer position, forward hope.

## The next-chapter hook

End with anticipation. Patterns:
- "Ready for [next chapter] whenever you are — [one-sentence tease]. [Emoji]"
- "Up next: [next chapter] — [tease]. [Emoji]"
- "[Next chapter] is coming — [what to expect]. [Emoji]"

Make the next chapter sound essential, not just sequential.
