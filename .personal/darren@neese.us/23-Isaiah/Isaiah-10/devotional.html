Isaiah 10:1–4

The Woe Aimed at the Lawmakers

"Woe to those who decree unrighteous decrees, who write misfortune, which they have prescribed, to rob the needy of justice, and to take what is right from the poor of My people, that widows may be their prey, and that they may rob the fatherless."
Isaiah 10:1–2

This is the fourth woe in a chain that began back in chapter 5 — and it's the most chilling. The first three were aimed at greed, drunkenness, and arrogance. This one is aimed at government. At the people holding the pen.

These rulers didn't just tolerate oppression. They didn't just look the other way. They wrote it down. They made oppression official — gave evil the full weight, protection, and permanence of law, so that crushing the defenseless was no longer a crime. It was policy.

And here's the part that should stop us cold: when evil is codified, the conscience of a nation goes to sleep. Nobody feels guilty obeying the law. The law is the moral baseline for most people. So when the law itself becomes the instrument of injustice, an entire population can participate in evil while feeling righteous about it.

That's the machinery Isaiah dismantles in two verses.

The verse that stopped you

"Those Who Decree Unrighteous Decrees"

Featured Verse · Isaiah 10:1
חֹקְקִים חִקְקֵי־אָוֶן
choqeqim chiqqei-aven — "the decree-ers who decree decrees of aven"

The Hebrew is a deliberate, almost mocking wordplay — Isaiah stacks the same root on itself to make the lawmakers sound like a machine stamping out injustice. Two words carry the freight:

חָקַק
chaqaq
to engrave · carve · inscribe

Ancient laws weren't typed and amended — they were cut into stone or metal. The word implies permanence and authority. It's the same root behind God's chuqqim, His statutes. These men took the divine act of law-giving — the thing God did at Sinai with His own finger on stone — and used it to engrave wickedness into the nation's foundation. A counterfeit Sinai. Human fingers carving anti-commandments.

אָוֶן
aven
iniquity · emptiness · sorrow-producing nothingness

English flattens this word. It's the root in "Beth-aven" — "house of nothingness," the prophets' insult-name for Bethel after it housed idols. These aren't just "unjust laws." They are laws that manufacture sorrow and are, at bottom, hollow — engraved with all the ceremony of permanence, yet weightless before God.

גָּזַל
gazal
to rob by violence · to tear away

Verse 2's verb. Isaiah says they use legislation to gazal justice from the poor. Robbery with a gavel instead of a knife.

"What will you do in the day of punishment... To whom will you flee for help? And where will you leave your glory?"
Isaiah 10:3

The lawmakers wrote laws to protect themselves. Isaiah asks: who wrote a law that can protect you from God?

The pattern in 2026

Innocent Blood, Celebrated

You named the parallel, and Scripture itself makes the bridge — we don't have to force it.

The defenseless ones in Isaiah's list — the poor, the widow, the fatherless — are the people with no voice and no power, the ones God explicitly claims as His own to defend (Psalm 68:5). The most voiceless and powerless human being in any society is the child not yet born. When a nation moves from permitting the shedding of innocent blood to codifying it — and then to celebrating it — it has walked the exact staircase Isaiah 10:1 describes:

1
Evil practiced
Individual sin, hidden, conscience still active
2
Evil legalized
Engraved into law — chaqaq — given permanence and protection
3
Evil celebrated
The national conscience is seared — approval is the final stage

Paul saw the final step coming:

"...who, knowing the righteous judgment of God, that those who practice such things are deserving of death, not only do the same but also approve of those who practice them."
Romans 1:32

The celebration is the tell. Approval is the last stage of a seared national conscience.

And the Old Testament has a name for institutionalized child sacrifice: Molech. Israel didn't just fall into it — they built infrastructure for it in the Valley of Hinnom, and Psalm 106:38 says the land was "polluted with blood."

JC
Jonathan Cahn

Has spent years tracing exactly this pattern — ancient Israel's template repeating in America: the same sins, the same defiance, even the same celebratory spirit — and the same God who sends warnings before judgment. The pattern isn't coincidence. It's a case-law precedent, and heaven's court keeps records.

But hold this too: Isaiah 10 isn't written to make us despair over the nation. It's written to show us who's actually running history — which is where the chapter takes its stunning turn.

🪓
Isaiah 10:5–19

The Axe That Thought It Was the Lumberjack

Verse 5 pivots: "Woe to Assyria, the rod of My anger."

God says something almost scandalous: Assyria — the brutal, pagan superpower — is His tool. He's sending it against "an hypocritical nation" (v. 6) to discipline His own people. But Assyria doesn't know it's a tool. Verse 7: "Yet he does not mean so." Assyria thinks it's conquering by its own genius.

So God poses one of the great rhetorical questions in Scripture:

"Shall the axe boast itself against him who chops with it?"
Isaiah 10:15
CM
Chuck Missler

Loved this verse — he called it the ultimate corrective to every empire's press release. The axe doesn't swing itself. Nations, parliaments, courts, and superpowers are instruments in a hand they cannot see, and the moment the instrument starts boasting, God sets a termination date.

Verses 16–19: when God is finished using Assyria, He burns it like a forest fire — so few trees left "a child may write them."

This is the comfort hiding inside the chapter: the same sovereignty that allows unjust lawmakers their season also sets the limit of that season. No decree of aven outlives its allotted time. Pharaoh learned it. Assyria learned it. Babylon learned it. Rome learned it.

🐍
Pulling back the curtain

The Unseen Layer

Laws that prey on the defenseless aren't merely bad policy — they're the signature of the one Jesus called "a murderer from the beginning" (John 8:44). Ephesians 6:12 says our wrestling isn't against flesh and blood but against rulers and authorities in heavenly places — and it's striking that Paul uses governmental vocabulary for the demonic realm.

The enemy has always sought to capture the engraving pen, because one wicked law does more damage than a thousand individual sins — it disciples a population into calling evil good (Isaiah 5:20, the woe right before this sequence began).

Which means the believer's primary weapon isn't outrage. It's the throne room. "First of all... prayers, intercessions... for kings and all who are in authority" (1 Timothy 2:1–2).

Isaiah 10:20–27

The Remnant Returns

Right in the middle of judgment, verse 21: "The remnant will return, the remnant of Jacob, to the Mighty God."

שְׁאָר יָשׁוּב
She'ar-Yashuv — "a remnant shall return"
The name of Isaiah's own son · Isaiah 7:3

Isaiah had been walking around Jerusalem for years with a living sermon holding his hand. God never loses His faithful core. And notice where the remnant returns to: El Gibbor, "Mighty God" — the exact title given to the Child of Isaiah 9:6 just a few verses earlier. The remnant doesn't return to national greatness. It returns to a Person.

"...the yoke will be destroyed because of the anointing."
Isaiah 10:27

Oppression has an expiration date — and it's stamped with Messiah's name.

🥾
Isaiah 10:28–34

The March That Stops

The chapter closes with a cinematic sequence — pure geography. Isaiah names the towns falling one by one as Assyria's army marches the ridge route south toward Jerusalem, panic spreading village by village:

  • Aiath — the invasion crosses in
  • Migron · Michmash — supplies stored, the pass crossed
  • Geba · Ramah — Gibeah of Saul flees
  • Anathoth · Madmenah · Gebim — village after village in panic
  • Nob — close enough to shake his fist at Mount Zion (v. 32)
  • ⛔ "Behold, the Lord... will lop off the bough with terror" — v. 33
BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Walks this passage like a tour guide, because that's what it is — a real route over real ridges, the terror traveling town by town down the map until the invader stands within sight of the Temple Mount.

History records what happened next: 185,000 Assyrian soldiers dead in one night outside Jerusalem's walls (Isaiah 37:36). The axe got within sight of the city of God — and the Lumberjack took it back.

Where we stand

Closing Reflection

Isaiah 10 is for believers living in a nation where injustice carries a legal seal and a celebration party. It tells us three things:

God sees

Every engraved decree of aven — and He has already asked its authors, "What will you do in the day of visitation?"

God governs

Even the powers that don't know His name. No axe boasts forever.

God keeps

A remnant whose hope was never the legislature anyway — but El Gibbor, the Mighty God who stops armies at the city limits.

So we grieve what's celebrated, we intercede for those holding the pen, we defend the voiceless with our own hands and voices — and we refuse despair, because the yoke is already scheduled for destruction "because of the anointing."
