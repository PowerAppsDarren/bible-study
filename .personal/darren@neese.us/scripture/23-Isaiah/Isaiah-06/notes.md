# 📖 Isaiah 6 — "The Year Heaven Tore Open"
### A Deep Devotional Study

> *Featured angle (from today's reading): "THE VAST SPECTACLE OF GOD'S THRONE ROOM!"*

---

## 🔥 Setting the Stage — "In the Year That King Uzziah Died"

Don't skip past verse 1. Isaiah is a literary master, and he opens with a *dateline* on purpose.

> *"In the year that King Uzziah died, I saw the Lord sitting upon a throne, high and lifted up, and his train filled the temple."* (Isaiah 6:1, KJV)

Uzziah reigned 52 years. **Fifty-two years.** That's longer than most people in Judah had been *alive*. He was the political constant. The economy boomed under him, the army was strong, the borders were secure (2 Chronicles 26). And then — he died. Slowly. Of leprosy. Because he walked into the Temple and tried to burn incense like he was a priest, and God struck him at the altar (2 Chr. 26:16-21).

So the throne of Judah is suddenly empty. The man is gone. The certainty is gone. The handrail Isaiah's whole generation leaned on — gone.

**That's exactly when Isaiah sees the *real* throne.**

Dr. Bill Creasy points out that this is one of the most masterful literary moves in the Hebrew Bible: the earthly king who *defiled* the temple dies, and in the same breath we see the heavenly King whose *robe* — just His robe — *fills* the temple. The contrast is the whole sermon. When your earthly anchors die, your eyes finally lift.

---

## ☁️ The Throne — "High and Lifted Up"

The Hebrew is staggering. "High and lifted up" is **רָם וְנִשָּׂא** (*ram v'nisa*). Isaiah uses that exact phrase only one other place in his book — **Isaiah 52:13**, about the *Suffering Servant* who "shall be exalted and extolled, and be very high." Same phrase. Same exaltation.

The throne Isaiah sees in chapter 6 and the Cross in chapter 53 are the **same lifting**.

And **John 12:41** — read this slowly — says of Jesus:

> *"These things said Esaias, when he saw his glory, and spake of him."*

John is telling you flat-out: the One on the throne in Isaiah 6 is **Jesus**. Chuck Missler used to lean on this verse like a hammer — the Old Testament is the New Testament concealed, the New Testament is the Old Testament revealed. The pre-incarnate Christ is the One whose robe fills the temple. Isaiah saw Him. That's a "wow factor" moment Missler would have spent twenty minutes on.

---

## 🔥 The Seraphim — The Burning Ones

> *"Above it stood the seraphims: each one had six wings; with twain he covered his face, and with twain he covered his feet, and with twain he did fly."* (Isaiah 6:2)

The word is **שָׂרָף** (*saraph*) — literally "**burning one**." Same root used in **Numbers 21:6** for the "fiery serpents" in the wilderness. These aren't fluffy cherubs from a Renaissance painting. These are beings on fire.

Six wings. Two cover the face — because even *they* cannot look directly at His glory. Two cover the feet — a posture of submission and modesty before holiness. Two for flight — for instant obedience.

Pause on that. **Sinless beings, who have never known a moment of rebellion, cannot look at the face of God without shielding themselves.** And we wonder why Isaiah falls apart in three more verses.

Perry Stone has taught for years that the heavenly beings around the throne are *categories* — seraphim here, cherubim in Ezekiel 1 and 10, the four living creatures in Revelation 4 — and they're not interchangeable. The seraphim are the *worship-and-purification* order. Notice it's a seraph that brings the coal in verse 6. Burning ones handle burning things.

---

## 🎵 "Holy, Holy, Holy" — The Only Attribute Tripled

> *"And one cried unto another, and said, Holy, holy, holy, is the LORD of hosts: the whole earth is full of his glory."* (Isaiah 6:3)

In Hebrew, repetition is emphasis. Saying something twice intensifies it. Saying it **three times** maxes out the language. There is no fourth tier.

**קָדוֹשׁ קָדוֹשׁ קָדוֹשׁ** (*qadosh qadosh qadosh*).

God is never called "love, love, love" or "mercy, mercy, mercy" or "just, just, just." He is *holy, holy, holy.* This is the only attribute lifted to the superlative-of-superlatives. Holiness is the throne-room atmosphere. Everything else — love, mercy, justice, wrath — flows *out* of His holiness.

And then John, in **Revelation 4:8**, sees four living creatures around the same throne saying the same thing — *"Holy, holy, holy, Lord God Almighty"* — in real time. Isaiah saw it in 740 BC. John saw it ~AD 95. The worship has not stopped. It is happening right now while you read this sentence.

That's the spectacle. The throne room you're asking about is not a memory. It's a *current event.*

---

## 🌍 "The Whole Earth Is Full of His Glory"

Don't miss the second half of verse 3. The seraphim aren't just praising the throne room — they're declaring something about *the dirt outside your window*.

The Hebrew word for "glory" is **כָּבוֹד** (*kavod*), and its root meaning is "weight, heaviness." God's glory is His manifest *weightiness* — His presence pressing into the visible world.

The seraphim are saying: *the whole earth is heavy with Him.* The throne room isn't sealed off in a far galaxy. Heaven and earth are not as separated as we think. Jonathan Cahn often teaches that in Hebrew thought, the *kavod* of God is what fell on Sinai, what filled the tabernacle, what departed the temple in Ezekiel 10, and what will fill the New Jerusalem in Revelation 21. It's the same weight, the same Person, the same Presence, all through the Bible.

So when Isaiah looks at the throne, and then the seraphim point him at the earth — he gets it. There is nowhere God is not.

---

## 💥 The Shaking and the Smoke

> *"And the posts of the door moved at the voice of him that cried, and the house was filled with smoke."* (Isaiah 6:4)

The doorposts — the heaviest, most fixed architectural element in the temple — **shake** at the *voice* of a created being praising God. Not at God's voice. At the *seraph's* voice. That's how much power is in a worship word spoken in the throne room.

And the house fills with smoke. Echo of Exodus 19 at Sinai. Echo of 1 Kings 8 when Solomon's temple is dedicated. Echo of Revelation 15:8 — *"the temple was filled with smoke from the glory of God."* Same pattern, same Presence, every time He shows up in full.

Dr. John Barnett would point out the consistency: God's glory always comes with smoke and shaking. When something in the spirit shakes today — when culture cracks, when economies tremble, when the foundations move — biblically that's not chaos. **That's a glory pattern.** Hebrews 12:26-27: *"Yet once more I shake not the earth only, but also heaven... that those things which cannot be shaken may remain."*

---

## 💔 "Woe Is Me!" — The Right Response to the Throne Room

> *"Then said I, Woe is me! for I am undone; because I am a man of unclean lips, and I dwell in the midst of a people of unclean lips: for mine eyes have seen the King, the LORD of hosts."* (Isaiah 6:5)

Here's the diagnostic test for whether someone has actually encountered the holy God: **they fall apart, not up.**

The Hebrew **נִדְמֵיתִי** (*nidmeiti*) — translated "undone" — means *"I am ruined, silenced, cut off, destroyed."* Isaiah doesn't say "wow, what a beautiful experience." He says "I'm done. I'm dead. I'm wrecked."

Compare:
- **Job 42:5-6** — *"Now mine eye seeth thee. Wherefore I abhor myself, and repent in dust and ashes."*
- **Daniel 10:8** — *"there remained no strength in me: for my comeliness was turned in me into corruption."*
- **Revelation 1:17** — John, the disciple Jesus loved, sees the glorified Christ and falls *"at his feet as dead."*

Every single person in Scripture who actually sees God collapses. The modern "I had an encounter with God" testimony that ends with "and I felt so warm and affirmed" deserves at least one raised eyebrow. The biblical pattern is *collapse, then commission.*

John Bevere has built decades of teaching on this — the *fear of the Lord* is not a starter doctrine you graduate from. It's the *foundation* of every encounter. Proverbs 9:10. Isaiah 6 is what holy fear looks like when the curtain pulls back.

---

## 🔥❄️ THE VERSE — The Coal on the Lips

Since you came for the **vast spectacle**, let's slow down at the moment where the spectacle bends down to one man.

> *"Then flew one of the seraphims unto me, having a live coal in his hand, which he had taken with the tongs from off the altar: And he laid it upon my mouth, and said, Lo, this hath touched thy lips; and thine iniquity is taken away, and thy sin purged."* (Isaiah 6:6-7)

Look at the choreography. The throne is high and lifted up. The seraphim are aflame. The doorposts are shaking. And then **one of these burning ones flies down to the floor where a wrecked prophet is lying face-down**, with a coal *the seraph itself can't hold barehanded* (he uses tongs from the altar), and **touches the man's lips with it.**

The Hebrew word for "live coal" is **רִצְפָּה** (*ritzpah*) — a glowing, burning stone. From the altar — the place of *atonement*. This is grace from the fire. Sin doesn't get *covered* here; it gets **burned out**.

The seraph declares two things over Isaiah:
- **עָוֹן** (*avon*) — iniquity, the crookedness, the twist — *taken away* (lifted off, removed)
- **חַטָּאת** (*chatat*) — the sin, the missing-the-mark — *purged* (atoned for, covered, propitiated)

Both Hebrew words for the human problem. Both resolved in one touch from the altar.

Now zoom out. **What is the altar?** It's where the substitutionary sacrifice burned. Whose substitutionary sacrifice does every Old Testament altar prefigure? *Jesus.* Hebrews 13:10 — *"We have an altar."* The coal that purges Isaiah is a foreshadow of the cross that purges you.

And here's the part I cannot get over: Isaiah did not ask for cleansing. He confessed his uncleanness and *that was it.* He did not perform, did not earn, did not work it off. The throne room moved toward him. **Heaven flies coals to confessed sinners.**

The spectacle of God's throne room is vast — but it bends.

---

## 📣 "Whom Shall I Send?" — The Commission

> *"Also I heard the voice of the Lord, saying, Whom shall I send, and who will go for us? Then said I, Here am I; send me."* (Isaiah 6:8)

Notice the plural — *"who will go for **us**."* Same plural as Genesis 1:26 ("let **us** make man"). Trinity-shaped language at the throne. The One sits on the throne, the Spirit is the smoke filling the house, and the Son is the One John 12:41 says Isaiah is looking at. The whole Godhead is in this room.

And notice the *order*: cleansing first, calling second. Isaiah didn't volunteer until after the coal. You cannot be sent until you've been purged. Every premature ministry burnout in the modern church is a person who skipped verse 7 and ran to verse 8.

The Hebrew **הִנְנִי** (*hineni*) — "Here am I" — is the same word Abraham used at the binding of Isaac (Genesis 22:1), Moses used at the burning bush (Exodus 3:4), and Samuel used in the night (1 Samuel 3:4). It's the word of total availability. *"All of me, ready, oriented to You."*

---

## ⚠️ The Hard Commission — A Word Most Pulpits Skip

Verses 9-13 are where most sermons quietly stop quoting. Because God's commission to Isaiah is **brutal**:

> *"Go, and tell this people, Hear ye indeed, but understand not; and see ye indeed, but perceive not. Make the heart of this people fat, and make their ears heavy, and shut their eyes..."* (Isaiah 6:9-10)

Isaiah is being sent to *preach to a nation that will not listen*, and the preaching itself will harden them further.

This passage is so heavy that **it is quoted in all four Gospels and Acts and Romans** — more than almost any other Old Testament passage:
- Matthew 13:14-15 (Jesus, on why He speaks in parables)
- Mark 4:12
- Luke 8:10
- John 12:40 (right before John says Isaiah saw *His* glory)
- Acts 28:26-27 (Paul, to the Jews in Rome)
- Romans 11:8

The pattern: **when a culture has had enough light and refused it, more preaching doesn't soften — it hardens.** This is the spiritual law underneath everything happening in the West right now. The Gospel has been preached on every corner for 200 years in this country. The current rejection isn't happening because people *haven't heard.* It's happening because they've heard, refused, and the refusal calcifies with every additional hearing.

This is sobering. It's also why faithful preachers keep preaching even when it looks like nothing is working — because the *remnant* is being identified by the same word that's hardening the rebels.

## 🌿 The Holy Seed in the Stump

> *"But yet in it shall be a tenth, and it shall return, and shall be eaten: as a teil tree, and as an oak, whose substance is in them, when they cast their leaves: so the holy seed shall be the substance thereof."* (Isaiah 6:13)

The chapter that opens with the most overwhelming spectacle in the Old Testament closes with a **stump**.

Judgment is coming. The tree comes down. But there's a stump — and *in the stump is the holy seed.* The Hebrew **זֶרַע קֹדֶשׁ** (*zera qodesh*) — "holy seed" — is messianic vocabulary. Same conceptual line that runs from Genesis 3:15 (the seed of the woman) through Isaiah 11:1 (*"there shall come forth a rod out of the stem of Jesse, and a Branch shall grow out of his roots"*).

The throne room scene ends with a *promise that the Messiah will come from the burned-down stump of Judah.* Which is exactly what happened. Bethlehem, a few miles from where Isaiah stood, would be the place. About 700 years later.

The vast spectacle has a long arc. God shows Isaiah the throne, gives him the commission, warns him the nation will fall — and then assures him **the Seed survives the fire.**

## 🕊️ Closing Reflection — Standing in the Smoke

You came for the vast spectacle of the throne room. Here it is:

- A throne so high a robe-hem fills a temple
- Burning beings who cannot look at His face
- A worship so loud the foundations shake
- A smoke so thick it floods the house
- A holiness so absolute the prophet collapses
- A mercy so personal the coal flies to the floor
- A commission so weighty it spans 700 years to the Cross
- A seed so faithful the stump still grows

And here's what to do with it: **this is the throne you pray to.** Every "Our Father in heaven" reaches *that* room. Hebrews 4:16 says you can come *boldly* — not flippantly, *boldly* — to *that* throne of grace. The seraphim cover their faces; you have an invitation to draw near. Not because you're worthy. Because the coal already touched the cross, and the cross already touched you.

The spectacle is not behind glass. The smoke is not in a museum. The throne is *now*, the worship is *now*, the holiness is *now*, the offer to send and use you is *now*.

**Hineni.** Say it back to Him today.
