🌱 Every tree fell — one stump sprouted.
Chapter 10 ended with the whole forest chopped down. Chapter 11 opens with the one shoot the axe never saw coming.
Isaiah 11:1
🌱  One Shoot from a Dead Stump

You have to read the last line of chapter 10 to feel the force of the first line of chapter 11. Isaiah 10 ends with God taking an axe to a forest — proud Assyria, and proud Judah’s leadership with it, leveled to the ground. Nothing left standing but stumps. Then, with no chapter break in Isaiah’s mind:

“There shall come forth a Rod from the stem of Jesse, and a Branch shall grow out of his roots.”
Isaiah 11:1

Notice the word God chooses: not “from the stem of David” — from the stem of Jesse, David’s father. Why reach back a generation behind the king? Because the royal house of David had become a glorious tree — and God is about to chop that tree down too. The line will be cut back so far it looks like a peasant farmer’s stump again, back to where it started before there was ever a throne. Jesse, not David.

And out of that dead-looking stump — one green shoot. The whole hope of the world riding on a sprig the world would step over. That is exactly how God works, and exactly how Bethlehem looked the night it happened.

❦   ❧
Featured Verse · Isaiah 11:1
חֹטֶר · גֵּזע · נֵצֶר
choter · geza · netzer — twig · stump · the Branch

English gives us soft garden words — “rod,” “stem,” “branch.” The Hebrew is sharper, and the third word hides a secret the New Testament springs on you 700 years later.

חֹטֶר choter
a twig · a single tender shoot

Not a sturdy bough — a thin green sprig you could snap with two fingers. The Messiah’s first appearance is deliberately unimpressive: “He grew up before Him as a tender plant, and as a root out of dry ground” (Isaiah 53:2).

גֵּזע geza
a stump · a cut-down stock

What’s left after the chopping — a tree that looks finished, written off. The line of David cut down to a stump, the kingship gone — and God grows His King out of the part everyone assumed was dead.

נֵצֶר netzer
a sprout · the Branch

Here’s the secret. Netzer becomes a title — the Branch — passed prophet to prophet (Jeremiah 23:5; Zechariah 3:8; 6:12). And from netzer comes Natzeret — Nazareth. Matthew 2:23: He settled there “that it might be fulfilled… He shall be called a Nazarene.” The Branch grew up in Branch-town.

By verse 10 the upgrade lands: He is no longer just the shoot that comes out of the roots — He is the Root. Jesus said the same impossible thing: “I am the Root and the Offspring of David” (Revelation 22:16). His own ancestor and descendant. That only makes sense if the Branch is also God.
Isaiah 11:2
🕎  The Sevenfold Spirit

What makes this shoot a King is the Spirit who rests on Him — Hebrew nuach, settles, stays, makes a home. The restless Dove finally found a branch to land on and never leave (John 1:32–33). Isaiah names one Spirit seven ways — a central lamp and three matched pairs. The rabbis pictured it as a menorah: one stem, seven flames, one light.

🔥 The Spirit of the LORD — the central lamp, the very Spirit of YHWH
Wisdom — chokmah · skill to live and rule rightly
Understanding — binah · discernment between things that look alike
Counsel — etsah · wisdom to decide and to plan
Might — gevurah · power to carry the plan through
Knowledge — da’at · intimate knowing of God, not just facts
The Fear of the LORD — yir’at YHWH · reverent awe — and v.3 says He delights in it
Chuck Missler

John picks the seven lamps right back up: “the seven Spirits before His throne” (Rev 1:4), the “seven lamps of fire” (4:5), the Lamb with “seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven Spirits of God” (5:6). Not seven Spirits — the one Spirit in His fullness, resting on Jesus without measure (John 3:34).

Isaiah 11:3–5
⚖️  The King Who Doesn’t Judge by the Feed
“He shall not judge by the sight of His eyes, nor decide by the hearing of His ears; but with righteousness He shall judge the poor…”
Isaiah 11:3–4

Sit with how radical this is in 2026. Our whole civilization judges by the two things this King refuses to judge by: the sight of the eyes (optics, the edited clip, the deepfake) and the hearing of the ears (the rumor, the hot take, trial-by-timeline). Here comes a King who can’t be fooled by the footage and won’t be swayed by the narrative — who finally gives the poor and the meek a verdict that’s actually right.

And the same mouth is a weapon: “with the breath of His lips He shall slay the wicked” (v.4). Paul grabs this exact line for the end (2 Thessalonians 2:8); John films it — the rider whose mouth holds a sharp sword (Revelation 19:15). Isaiah saw the rod of His mouth; John watched it land.

Isaiah 11:6–9
🐺  The Peaceable Kingdom

Then the picture every weary adult secretly aches for — creation’s food chain unplugged, a toddler running the petting zoo:

🐺🐑
wolf & lamb
🐆🐐
leopard & goat
🦁🐄
lion & calf
👶🐍
child & cobra

This isn’t a metaphor for “people getting along.” Isaiah means the created order itself — the curse of Genesis 3 rolled back. And the reason peace floods the world (v.9) isn’t treaties: “the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD as the waters cover the sea.” Violence drains out to the exact degree the knowledge of God floods in. Habakkuk heard the same ocean coming (Habakkuk 2:14) — the sea isn’t sprinkled with water; it is water, all the way down.

🐍 The hand on the serpent’s den

Don’t rush past v.8: “the weaned child shall put his hand in the viper’s den.” The word is nachash — the serpent of Genesis 3. In Eden, God told the serpent “He shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise His heel” (Genesis 3:15). Now: a child reaches a bare hand into the serpent’s lair — and nothing happens. The curse running in reverse. The head crushed, the venom gone, the strike-zone turned playground.

Paul promises the church its share of that stomp: “The God of peace will crush Satan under your feet shortly” (Romans 16:20) — the same verb, the same enemy, given to us. The drama that opened with a serpent and a tree ends with a child safe at the serpent’s hole and a tree of life whose leaves heal the nations (Revelation 22:2). Same characters, opposite ending.

Isaiah 11:10–12
🚩  The Banner the Nations Run To

Twice the Branch becomes a banner — Hebrew nes, a signal-pole raised on a hill so scattered, frightened people can see the rally point and run. That word has a history: God told Moses to lift a bronze serpent on a pole — a nes — and “everyone who is bitten, when he looks at it, shall live” (Numbers 21:8). Jesus claimed it: “As Moses lifted up the serpent… even so must the Son of Man be lifted up” (John 3:14); “I, if I am lifted up… will draw all peoples to Myself” (John 12:32).

“…there shall be a root of Jesse… in Him the Gentiles shall hope.”
Romans 15:12, quoting Isaiah 11:10

If you’re not ethnically Jewish, that’s you — written into Isaiah’s hope 700 years before you were born. And v.11 adds a marker: God recovers His people “the second time.” The first was out of Egypt; a second, end-of-days regathering from “the four corners of the earth” is still on the calendar — which is why teachers like Jonathan Cahn read the modern return of Israel as this very pattern resurfacing.

Isaiah 11:13–16
🛣️  The Highway Home

The chapter lands on a road built for the journey home: “a highway… as it was for Israel in the day that he came up from Egypt” (v.16). Isaiah frames the whole future as a second Exodus — God parting waters again, making a road through the impossible.

Dr. Bill Creasy

Chapter 11 deliberately bookends chapter 10. Chapter 10 was a terror-march of an army coming down the ridge to destroy Jerusalem. Chapter 11 is a joy-march on a highway going home. Same map, reversed direction — invasion turned into homecoming. Isaiah is too good a writer for that to be an accident.

🕊️  Where we stand

Isaiah 11 is for anyone standing in the rubble of chapter 10 — a world where the forest got chopped, the powerful judge by optics, and the strong still eat the weak. It hands you three things to hold:

The stump lives. When everything you trusted is cut down to a dead-looking stub, that’s the soil God grows His best work out of.

The King is true. One day the verdict that matters won’t be decided by footage or rumor, but in perfect righteousness, by a King who can’t be spun.

The serpent’s done. The head is already crushed. A child will play where the viper struck — and the knowledge of God will flood the earth like the sea.

So we live as people who already know the ending. The banner is up. The Branch grew. The Gentiles came. The peaceable kingdom isn’t wishful thinking — it’s a destination with a highway already under construction.
