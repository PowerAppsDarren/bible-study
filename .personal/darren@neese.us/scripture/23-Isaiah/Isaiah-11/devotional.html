Isaiah 11:1

One Shoot from a Dead Stump

You have to read the last line of chapter 10 to feel the force of the first line of chapter 11. Isaiah 10:33–34 ends with God taking an axe to a forest: "He will cut down the thickets of the forest with iron, and Lebanon will fall." Proud Assyria — and proud Judah's leadership with it — leveled to the ground. Nothing left standing but stumps.

Then, no chapter break in Isaiah's mind:

"There shall come forth a Rod from the stem of Jesse, and a Branch shall grow out of his roots."
Isaiah 11:1

Notice the word God chooses: not "from the stem of David" — from the stem of Jesse, David's father. Why reach back a generation behind the king? Because by Isaiah's day the royal house of David had become a glorious tree — and God is about to chop that tree down too. The line will be cut back so far it'll look like a peasant farmer's stump again, back to where it started before there was ever a throne. Jesse, not David. The dynasty reduced to a name in Bethlehem.

And out of that dead-looking stump — one green shoot. The whole hope of the world riding on a sprig the world would step over. That is exactly how God works, and exactly how Bethlehem looked the night it happened.

The verse the whole chapter grows from

"A Branch Shall Grow Out of His Roots"

Featured Verse · Isaiah 11:1
חֹטֶר · גֵּזַע · נֵצֶר
choter · geza · netzer — twig · stump · the Branch

English gives us soft garden words — "rod," "stem," "branch." The Hebrew is sharper and stranger, and the third word hides a secret the New Testament will spring on you 700 years later.

חֹטֶר
choter
a twig · a single tender shoot

Not a sturdy bough — a thin green sprig, the kind you could snap with two fingers. The Messiah's first appearance is deliberately unimpressive. "He grew up before Him as a tender plant, and as a root out of dry ground" (Isaiah 53:2). The same prophet, same image — fragile, easy to despise, easy to miss.

גֵּזַע
geza
a stump · a cut-down stock

A geza is what's left after the chopping — a tree that looks finished, dead, written off. Job 14:8 uses it of a stump whose root is old in the ground. The point is brutal and beautiful: the line of David would be cut down to a stump, the kingship gone — and God grows His King out of the part everyone assumed was dead.

נֵצֶר
netzer
a sprout · the Branch

Here's the secret. Netzer becomes a title — the Branch — a Messianic code-word the prophets pass hand to hand (Jeremiah 23:5; Zechariah 3:8; 6:12). And from netzer comes the town name Natzeret — Nazareth. Matthew 2:23 says Jesus settled there "that it might be fulfilled... He shall be called a Nazarene" — a verse with no single Old Testament source, because Matthew is pointing at the word, not one quote. The Branch grew up in Branch-town.

"And in that day there shall be a Root of Jesse, who shall stand as a banner to the people; for the Gentiles shall seek Him, and His resting place shall be glorious."
Isaiah 11:10

Watch the upgrade. In v.1 He is the shoot that comes out of the roots. By v.10 He is the Root — the source the whole tree grows from. Jesus said the same impossible thing about Himself: "I am the Root and the Offspring of David" (Revelation 22:16). Both his ancestor and his descendant. That sentence only makes sense if the Branch is also God.

Isaiah 11:2

The Sevenfold Spirit

What makes this shoot a King isn't pedigree — it's the Spirit who rests on Him. And Isaiah doesn't say "the Spirit came on Him" the way the Spirit came and went on the judges and Saul. He says the Spirit rests — nuach, settles down, stays, makes a home. The restless Dove finally found a branch to land on and never leave (John 1:32–33).

"The Spirit of the LORD shall rest upon Him, the Spirit of wisdom and understanding, the Spirit of counsel and might, the Spirit of knowledge and of the fear of the LORD."
Isaiah 11:2

Count them. One Spirit, named seven ways — a central Spirit of the LORD, then three matched pairs branching off. The rabbis pictured it as a menorah: one central stem, six lamps, all one light.

CM
Chuck Missler

Lit up at this verse, because John picks the seven lamps right back up: "the seven Spirits who are before His throne" (Revelation 1:4), the "seven lamps of fire burning before the throne" (4:5), and the Lamb with "seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven Spirits of God" (5:6). Missler's point: this isn't seven Spirits — it's the one Spirit in His fullness, the menorah of God. Isaiah saw the lampstand; John saw it burning before the throne; and it rests on Jesus without measure (John 3:34).

The last item in the list — the fear of the LORD — becomes the hinge for what He does with all this Spirit-given power in the very next verse.

Isaiah 11:3–5

The King Who Doesn't Judge by the Feed

"He shall not judge by the sight of His eyes, nor decide by the hearing of His ears; but with righteousness He shall judge the poor, and decide with equity for the meek of the earth."
Isaiah 11:3–4

Sit with how radical this is in 2026. We live in a civilization that judges by exactly the two things this King refuses to judge by: the sight of the eyes (optics, image, the photo, the edited clip, the deepfake) and the hearing of the ears (the rumor, the hot take, the trial-by-timeline, the thing "everybody's saying"). Our courts of public opinion run entirely on eyes and ears — on what looks true and what sounds true.

Here comes a King who can't be fooled by the footage and won't be swayed by the narrative, because He sees straight to the root. He finally gives the poor and the meek — the ones who always lose the optics war because they can't afford a PR team — a verdict that's actually right. For everyone who's ever been wronged and watched the powerful spin it, this verse is oxygen.

But the same mouth that vindicates the meek is a weapon against the wicked:

"He shall strike the earth with the rod of His mouth, and with the breath of His lips He shall slay the wicked."
Isaiah 11:4

No sword in His hand — the word of His mouth is the weapon. Paul grabs this exact line for the end of the age: the lawless one whom "the Lord will consume with the breath of His mouth" (2 Thessalonians 2:8). And John watches it land — the rider on the white horse from whose mouth "goes a sharp sword, that with it He should strike the nations" (Revelation 19:15). Isaiah saw the rod of His mouth; John filmed it.

Isaiah 11:6–9

The Peaceable Kingdom

Then the most famous picture in the chapter — the one every children's Bible illustrates and every weary adult secretly aches for. Creation's food chain unplugged. Predator and prey lying down together, and a toddler in charge of the petting zoo:

the wolf with the lamb
🐆🐐

🦁🐄
the lion with the calf
👶🐍
the child over the cobra's den
"...and a little child shall lead them... They shall not hurt nor destroy in all My holy mountain, for the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD as the waters cover the sea."
Isaiah 11:6, 9

This isn't a metaphor for "people getting along." Isaiah means it about the created order itself — the curse of Genesis 3 rolled back, death's appetite switched off. He says it again in 65:25. And notice the reason peace floods the world (v.9): not better policy, not disarmament treaties — "the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD." Violence drains out of the world to the exact degree the knowledge of God floods in. Where He is truly known, nothing hurts or destroys.

Habakkuk heard the same future roaring: "the earth will be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD, as the waters cover the sea" (Habakkuk 2:14). Two prophets, one ocean. The sea isn't sprinkled with water — it is water, all the way down. That's the saturation coming.

🐍
The layer under the layer

The Hand on the Serpent's Den

Don't rush past verse 8. Of all the pairs, Isaiah saves the loaded one for last:

"The nursing child shall play by the cobra's hole, and the weaned child shall put his hand in the viper's den."
Isaiah 11:8

The Hebrew word is nachash — the serpent. The same creature from Genesis 3. Back in Eden, God spoke the first promise of the gospel directly to the serpent: "He shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise His heel" (Genesis 3:15) — and ever since, the serpent has struck at the heel of every human child. Now look at Isaiah's image: a child reaching a bare hand into the serpent's lair — and nothing happens.

This is the curse of Eden running in reverse. The seed of the woman has crushed the head; the venom is gone; the strike-zone is now a playground. Paul promises the church its share of that very stomp: "The God of peace will crush Satan under your feet shortly" (Romans 16:20) — the Genesis 3 verb, aimed at the same enemy, given to us. And Jesus already handed His followers the down payment: "I give you authority to trample on serpents... and nothing shall by any means hurt you" (Luke 10:19).

PS
Perry Stone

Would point straight at the Hebrew here — nachash, the Eden serpent — and call Isaiah 11:8 the prophetic snapshot of the whole Bible's plotline closing the loop. The drama that opened with a serpent and a tree ends with a child safe at the serpent's hole and a tree of life whose leaves heal the nations (Revelation 22:2). Same characters, opposite ending.

So the peaceable kingdom isn't sentimental. It's a victory monument — built on a crushed head.

Isaiah 11:10–12

The Banner the Nations Run To

Twice in this section the Branch becomes a banner — Hebrew nes, a signal-pole raised on a hilltop so scattered, frightened people can see the rally point from miles off and run to it.

נֵס
nes — a banner · ensign · rally-signal
Isaiah 11:10, 12

That word has a history. When Israel was dying of snakebite in the wilderness, God told Moses to lift a bronze serpent on a pole — a nes — and "everyone who is bitten, when he looks at it, shall live" (Numbers 21:8). Jesus claimed that pole as a picture of His cross: "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of Man be lifted up" (John 3:14), and "I, if I am lifted up... will draw all peoples to Myself" (John 12:32). The Root of Jesse lifted up as the banner — exactly what Isaiah said: "the Gentiles shall seek Him."

Paul nails the lid on it. Closing his argument that the gospel was always meant for the nations, he quotes this verse:

"...there shall be a root of Jesse; and He who shall rise to reign over the Gentiles, in Him the Gentiles shall hope."
Romans 15:12, quoting Isaiah 11:10

If you're not ethnically Jewish, that's you — written into Isaiah's hope 700 years before you were born. You are part of why the banner went up.

And v.11 adds a detail worth marking: God says He will recover His people "the second time." The first recovery was out of Egypt. A second, end-of-days regathering from "the four corners of the earth" (v.12) is still on the calendar — which is why teachers like Jonathan Cahn read the modern return of Israel to its land as this very pattern resurfacing: the same God, the same promise, the same banner still raised.

🛣️
Isaiah 11:13–16

The Highway Home

The chapter lands on geography — old enemies reconciled (Ephraim and Judah quit envying each other, v.13), and a literal road built for the journey home:

"There will be a highway for the remnant of His people who will be left from Assyria, as it was for Israel in the day that he came up from the land of Egypt."
Isaiah 11:16

Read that comparison slowly: "as it was... from Egypt." Isaiah frames the whole future as a second Exodus — God parting waters again (v.15), drying the tongue of the sea, making a road through the impossible for people coming home. The first Exodus was the template; the real one is still ahead.

BC
Dr. Bill Creasy

Reads Isaiah as one unbroken literary masterpiece, and he'd flag the craftsmanship here: chapter 11 deliberately bookends the misery of chapter 10. Chapter 10 was a terror-march of an army coming down the ridge toward Jerusalem to destroy. Chapter 11 is a joy-march on a highway going home. Same map, reversed direction — invasion turned into homecoming. Isaiah is too good a writer for that to be an accident.

Where we stand

Closing Reflection

Isaiah 11 is for anyone standing in the rubble of chapter 10 — a world where the forest got chopped, the powerful judge by optics, and the strong still eat the weak. It hands you three things to hold:

The stump lives

When everything you trusted gets cut down to a dead-looking stub, that's not the end — that's the soil God grows His best work out of. He specializes in shoots from stumps.

The King is true

One day the verdict that matters won't be decided by the eyes and the ears — by footage or rumor — but in perfect righteousness, by a King who can't be spun.

The serpent's done

The head is already crushed. The day is coming when a child plays where the viper struck — and the knowledge of God floods the earth like the sea.

So we live as people who already know the ending. The banner is up. The Branch grew. The Gentiles came. And the same Spirit that rested on Him — wisdom, counsel, might, the fear of the LORD — is the Spirit poured out on everyone hidden in Him. The peaceable kingdom isn't wishful thinking. It's a destination with a highway already under construction.
