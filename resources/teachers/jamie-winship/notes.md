# Jamie Winship — Study Notes

Transformative summaries of Winship's frameworks, drawn from his freely available interviews and ministry pages (see [`links.md`](links.md)). These are study notes, not transcripts. Short direct quotes are in [`quotes.md`](quotes.md).

## The central thesis

All human conflict originates from **fear**, and fear originates from a **false view of God, of ourselves, and of others**. Until the false is exchanged for the true, real freedom is impossible. Winship developed this through decades of reconciliation work in active conflict zones — the same root, he argues, drives a marriage fight and a war.

## Identity vs. calling — *being* before *doing*

Winship sharply distinguishes **identity (being)** from **calling (doing)**. Identity must come first; calling flows out of it. He warns that fusing the two — deriving your worth from your role — is what destroys good people (he speaks especially of police officers, his own former vocation). "A good tree produces good fruit": the being determines the fruit, not the reverse.

## True self vs. false self

The **false self** is built from the lies of fear, guilt, and shame, and from the names the world assigns ("failure," "fraud," "not enough"). The **true self** is the identity God speaks over a person. Transformation is an **exchange**: surrender the false, receive the true. You don't *achieve* the true self; you *receive* it.

## Hearing God's voice — the Identity Prayer

Winship insists hearing God is simpler than believers make it: you already have the mind of Christ and the Spirit within. His pattern: **fix your eyes on Jesus, ask Him a direct question, and wait.** The signature question — the *Identity Prayer* — is some form of:

> "God, what do you call me when you talk about me?" / "What is my true name?"

The answer God gives reorders everything. This is the bridge from the renaming narratives of Scripture (Abram→Abraham, Jacob→Israel, Simon→Peter, Gideon the "mighty man of valor") to the reader's own life.

## True vs. real

A recurring distinction: circumstances, wounds, and fears are **real** — but what God says is **true**, and the true is more authoritative than the real. The disciple learns to live *from* the true rather than *from* the real. "What God says about you is more true than what is happening to you."

## The four elements of abiding

Winship frames abiding (staying with God, and staying with people) in four movements:

1. **Attention** — fixing on Jesus / being present
2. **Awareness** — perceiving what God and reality are actually showing
3. **Annunciation** — naming / declaring what is heard and true
4. **Action** — moving on it

Abiding is "the commitment to stay with" — the place where deep learning and change happen.

## Two worldviews — scarcity/conflict vs. connection/sufficiency

Behind every conflict sits a **worldview**. The **conflict (scarcity) worldview** says there is not enough — not enough time, resources, or worth — and so people compete, protect, and fight. The **connection (sufficiency) worldview**, grounded in who God is, rests on three convictions:

- *There is enough time.*
- *There are enough resources.*
- *You are enough.*

Living from sufficiency dissolves the drivers of conflict.

## Enemies aren't really enemies

From his reconciliation work: God commands love of enemies because, rightly seen through true identity, "they're not really your enemy." Conflict is downstream of fear and false identity on *both* sides; address the root and the enmity loses its fuel.

## "If you don't understand who God is, you'll never understand what He does"

Winship grounds identity in theology proper: a distorted picture of God's character is the taproot of fear. Get God right, and the false self loses its foundation. This is why his method is not self-esteem — the *source* of the true self is God's spoken word about you, not self-affirmation.

## Biographical thread (for context)

Dreamed of police work from age 13; came to faith at 17 through a nurse during a knee surgery; joined the D.C. force at 23. Grew disillusioned and began praying over his cases — the prayer-informed results drew repeated CIA interest and led to independent reconciliation work in conflict zones worldwide. A mentor's early counsel — *"keep your mouth shut for one year"* and listen — became seed for his theology of listening and abiding. (Source: Christianity Today *Where Ya From?* and Jennie Allen interviews; see [`links.md`](links.md).)
