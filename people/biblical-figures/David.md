# David

**Lived:** United-monarchy period; reign c. 1010–970 BC (a long reign of ~40 years; 2 Sam 5:4; 1 Kings 2:11). All dates are scholarly estimates derived from biblical regnal data and synchronisms, not fixed external chronology.
**Tradition / role:** Second king of united Israel (after Saul); founder of the Judahite royal dynasty; ancestor of the messianic line.
**Known for:** Shepherd-boy turned warrior-king, slayer of Goliath, recipient of the everlasting-dynasty covenant (2 Sam 7), and the figure most associated with Israel's Psalms.

Hebrew name: דָּוִד (*Dāwid*), conventionally taken to mean "beloved." Son of Jesse (Hebrew *Yishai*) of Bethlehem, of the tribe of Judah.

## Biography

**Origins (1 Sam 16; Ruth 4:17–22).** David is the youngest of Jesse's sons in Bethlehem, descended through Boaz and Ruth from Judah. Samuel, sent to Jesse after Saul's rejection, anoints David privately (1 Sam 16:1–13). David then enters Saul's service as a musician who soothes the troubled king (1 Sam 16:14–23).

**Goliath and rise (1 Sam 17–18).** David kills the Philistine champion Goliath with a sling, winning national fame. He forms a covenant friendship with Saul's son Jonathan (1 Sam 18:1–4; 20) and marries Saul's daughter Michal (1 Sam 18:20–27). His success — "Saul has slain his thousands, and David his ten thousands" (1 Sam 18:7) — provokes Saul's jealousy.

**Fugitive years (1 Sam 19–27).** Saul repeatedly seeks David's life. David flees, gathers a band of followers (1 Sam 22:1–2), and twice spares Saul when he could have killed him (1 Sam 24; 26), refusing to harm "the LORD's anointed." He later takes refuge among the Philistines under Achish of Gath and is given the town of Ziklag (1 Sam 27).

**Saul's death (1 Sam 31; 2 Sam 1).** Saul and his sons, including Jonathan, die at Mount Gilboa. David composes a lament for Saul and Jonathan (2 Sam 1:17–27).

**King at Hebron, then over all Israel (2 Sam 2–5).** David is anointed king over Judah at Hebron (2 Sam 2:1–4), reigning there ~7.5 years during a war with the house of Saul. After the deaths of Abner and Ish-bosheth, the northern tribes make him king over all Israel (2 Sam 5:1–5).

**Jerusalem and the ark (2 Sam 5–6).** David captures the Jebusite stronghold of Jerusalem and makes it his capital ("the city of David"). He brings the ark of the covenant up to Jerusalem (2 Sam 6), establishing the city as a political and cultic center.

**The Davidic covenant (2 Sam 7; 1 Chr 17).** When David proposes to build a temple, the prophet Nathan delivers God's promise instead: God will build David a "house" (dynasty), and David's throne will be established forever.

**Military expansion (2 Sam 8; 10).** David subdues surrounding peoples — Philistines, Moabites, Arameans, Ammonites, Edomites — extending Israel's territory and tribute.

**Sin with Bathsheba and Uriah (2 Sam 11–12).** David commits adultery with Bathsheba, wife of Uriah the Hittite, then arranges Uriah's death in battle to conceal it. Nathan confronts him with a parable; David confesses, but the child dies, and Nathan announces lasting consequences of the sword within David's own house. Bathsheba later bears Solomon.

**Family turmoil and Absalom's revolt (2 Sam 13–20).** Amnon rapes his half-sister Tamar; Absalom kills Amnon in revenge and is eventually estranged. Absalom mounts a rebellion that drives David from Jerusalem; the revolt ends with Absalom's death, which David mourns deeply (2 Sam 18:33). Sheba's revolt follows (2 Sam 20). These chapters present David's failures as a father and ruler candidly.

**The census (2 Sam 24; 1 Chr 21).** David orders a military census, regarded as sin; a plague follows. David buys the threshing floor of Araunah/Ornan the Jebusite and builds an altar — the site later associated with the temple (cf. 2 Chr 3:1).

**Succession and death (1 Kings 1–2; 1 Chr 28–29).** In David's old age, Adonijah claims the throne, but David, prompted by Bathsheba and Nathan, names Solomon as successor. David charges Solomon and dies after roughly 40 years' reign, buried in the city of David (1 Kings 2:10).

Chronicles (1 Chr 11–29) retells David's reign with heavy emphasis on his preparations for the temple and the organization of worship, and largely omits the Bathsheba affair and Absalom material — a difference in editorial focus rather than contradiction.

## Theological / canonical significance

**The Davidic covenant.** 2 Sam 7 (paralleled in 1 Chr 17 and celebrated in Ps 89 and Ps 132) promises David an enduring dynasty and throne. This unconditional dynastic promise becomes a backbone of OT royal and messianic hope, sustaining expectation of a coming anointed king from David's line (e.g., Isa 9:6–7; 11:1; Jer 23:5; Ezek 34:23–24).

**"A man after God's own heart."** 1 Sam 13:14 and Paul's citation in Acts 13:22 describe David this way. In context the phrase concerns God's choice of a king who would do God's will, not a claim of moral perfection — the narratives themselves record grave sins.

**Messianic typology.** The NT presents Jesus as the "Son of David" and heir of the Davidic promise: the genealogies (Matt 1; Luke 3:23–38), the annunciation ("the throne of his father David," Luke 1:32–33), and Paul's summary that Jesus was "of the seed of David according to the flesh" (Rom 1:3). Jesus debates the meaning of Ps 110:1 ("The LORD said unto my Lord") regarding David's son and lord (Matt 22:41–46).

**Apostolic use in Acts.** Peter cites Ps 16:8–11, arguing David spoke as a prophet of the resurrection since David himself died and was buried (Acts 2:25–36). Paul makes a parallel argument in Acts 13:34–37.

**David and the Psalms.** Seventy-three psalms carry the superscription *le-David* ("of/for/belonging to David"). The traditional view treats many as Davidic compositions; much critical scholarship reads the superscriptions as later editorial ascriptions and dates numerous psalms after David, while others defend a substantial Davidic core. The Hebrew preposition itself is ambiguous (authorship, dedication, or collection), and the views are presented here without adjudication. Regardless of authorship debates, David is consistently portrayed as a musician and patron of Israel's worship (1 Sam 16:18; 2 Sam 23:1; 1 Chr 16; Amos 6:5).

**Historicity and extrabiblical attestation.** The Tel Dan Stele (9th century BC, Aramaic), and on most readings the Mesha (Moabite) Stele, contain the phrase *bytdwd* — usually rendered "House of David" — widely cited as extrabiblical reference to a Davidic dynasty. A minority of scholars dispute the reading or its dynastic sense. The size and reach of David's kingdom as described in Samuel is also debated among historians and archaeologists; the views range from a substantial regional polity to a more modest chiefdom.

## Key works / key passages

- **Primary narrative:** 1 Samuel 16–31; 2 Samuel 1–24; 1 Kings 1–2.
- **Chronicler's account:** 1 Chronicles 11–29 (esp. temple preparations, 1 Chr 22; 28–29).
- **Davidic covenant:** 2 Samuel 7; 1 Chronicles 17; Psalm 89; Psalm 132.
- **Genealogy:** Ruth 4:17–22; Matthew 1:1–17; Luke 3:23–38.
- **Psalms ascribed to David (selected):** Ps 3; Ps 18 (cf. 2 Sam 22); Ps 23; Ps 51 (superscription linking it to the Bathsheba episode); Ps 110.
- **David's "last words":** 2 Samuel 23:1–7.
