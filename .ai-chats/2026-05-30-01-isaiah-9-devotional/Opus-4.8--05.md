## The Names of the Child-King (Isaiah 9:6)

- **Wonderful Counselor** — פֶּלֶא יוֹעֵץ *(Pele' Yo'etz)*
  *Pele'* = a *miracle*, something that breaks all categories. He doesn't just *give* wonderful advice — He **is** the Wonder who counsels. (Same root as the Angel's name in Judges 13:18.)

- **Mighty God** — אֵל גִּבּוֹר *(El Gibbor)*
  *El* = **God**, flat-out. Not a demigod or exalted man. Isaiah uses the *identical phrase* for Yahweh Himself one chapter later (10:21) — he knew exactly what he was claiming about this Child.

- **Everlasting Father** — אֲבִי־עַד *(Avi-'ad)*
  Literally *"Father of eternity."* Not saying the Son *is* the Father (the persons are distinct) — it's a Hebrew idiom: He is the source and possessor of the ages, fatherly toward His people **forever**.

- **Prince of Peace** — שַׂר־שָׁלוֹם *(Sar Shalom)*
  *Shalom* isn't just "no war" — it's **wholeness, everything-set-right**. He doesn't broker a ceasefire. He *is* the peace. (And note: 9:7 says *"of the increase... and peace there will be no end."*)

---

Some teachers also pull a **fifth** name out of the front of the verse:

- **Wonderful** *(on its own)* — read separately from "Counselor," giving the pattern **Wonderful / Counselor / Mighty God / Everlasting Father / Prince of Peace.** Most Hebrew scholars pair them as "Wonderful Counselor," but it's a legitimate reading worth knowing.

And don't miss the two verbs that set them up: a **Child is born** (His humanity — born down into Bethlehem) and a **Son is given** (His deity — the gift sent down from eternity). The names hang on both.
