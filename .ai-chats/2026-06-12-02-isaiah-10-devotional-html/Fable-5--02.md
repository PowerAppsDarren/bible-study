# 📖 Isaiah 10 — "When Evil Gets Codified"
### A Deep Devotional Study

---

## ⚖️ The Woe Aimed at the Lawmakers

> "Woe to those who decree unrighteous decrees, who write misfortune, which they have prescribed, to rob the needy of justice, and to take what is right from the poor of My people, that widows may be their prey, and that they may rob the fatherless." — **Isaiah 10:1-2**

This is the fourth woe in a chain that started back in chapter 5, and it's the most chilling one — because the first three were aimed at greed, drunkenness, and arrogance. This one is aimed at **government**. At the people holding the pen.

Notice the progression Isaiah describes. These rulers didn't just *tolerate* oppression. They didn't just *look the other way*. They **wrote it down**. They made oppression *official*. They gave evil the full weight, protection, and permanence of law — so that crushing the defenseless was no longer a crime. It was *policy*.

And here's the part that should stop us cold: when evil is codified, the conscience of a nation goes to sleep. Nobody feels guilty obeying the law. The law *is* the moral baseline for most people. So when the law itself becomes the instrument of injustice, an entire population can participate in evil while feeling righteous about it.

That's the machinery Isaiah is dismantling in two verses.

---

## ❄️ THE VERSE — "Those Who Decree Unrighteous Decrees"

Since this is what grabbed you, let's go deep on **10:1**.

The Hebrew is a deliberate, almost mocking wordplay: **חֹקְקִים חִקְקֵי־אָוֶן** (*choqeqim chiqqei-aven*) — literally "the decree-ers who decree decrees of *aven*."

Two words carry the freight:

**חָקַק (chaqaq)** — to engrave, to carve, to inscribe. In the ancient world, laws weren't typed and amended — they were *cut into stone or metal*. The word implies **permanence and authority**. It's the same root behind God's *chuqqim*, His statutes. So Isaiah is saying: these men took the divine act of law-giving — the thing God did at Sinai with His own finger on stone — and used it to engrave *wickedness* into the foundation of the nation. It's a counterfeit Sinai. Human fingers carving anti-commandments.

**אָוֶן (aven)** — iniquity, but with a flavor English misses: *emptiness, trouble, sorrow-producing nothingness*. It's the word in "Beth-aven" — "house of nothingness," the prophets' insult-name for Bethel after it housed idols. So these aren't just "unjust laws." They are laws that *manufacture sorrow* and are, at bottom, **hollow** — engraved with all the ceremony of permanence, yet weightless before God.

And look at verse 2's verb: **גָּזַל (gazal)** — to rob *by violence*, to tear away. Isaiah says they use legislation to *gazal* justice from the poor. Robbery with a gavel instead of a knife.

Then comes the question that ends the stanza, verse 3:

> "What will you do in the day of punishment... To whom will you flee for help? And where will you leave your glory?"

The lawmakers wrote laws to protect themselves. Isaiah asks: *who wrote a law that can protect you from God?*

---

## 🩸 The Pattern in 2026 — Innocent Blood, Celebrated

You named the parallel, and Scripture itself makes the bridge — we don't have to force it.

The defenseless ones in Isaiah's list — the poor, the widow, the fatherless — are the people with **no voice and no power**, the ones God explicitly claims as His own to defend (Psalm 68:5). The most voiceless and powerless human being in any society is the child not yet born. When a nation moves from *permitting* the shedding of innocent blood to *codifying* it — and then to **celebrating** it — it has walked the exact staircase Isaiah 10:1 describes: evil practiced, then evil legalized, then evil applauded.

Paul saw the final step coming:

> "...who, knowing the righteous judgment of God, that those who practice such things are deserving of death, not only do the same but also **approve of those who practice them**." — **Romans 1:32**

The celebration is the tell. Approval is the last stage of a seared national conscience.

And the Old Testament has a name for institutionalized child sacrifice: **Molech**. Israel didn't just fall into it — they built infrastructure for it in the Valley of Hinnom, and Psalm 106:38 says the land was "polluted with blood." **Jonathan Cahn** has spent years tracing exactly this pattern — ancient Israel's template repeating in America: the same sins, the same defiance, even the same celebratory spirit — and the same God who sends warnings before judgment. The pattern isn't a coincidence. It's a *case law precedent*, and heaven's court keeps records.

But hold this too: Isaiah 10 isn't written to make us despair over the nation. It's written to show us **who's actually running history** — which is where the chapter takes its stunning turn.

## 🪓 The Axe That Thought It Was the Lumberjack (vv. 5-19)

Verse 5 pivots: "Woe to Assyria, **the rod of My anger**."

God says something almost scandalous: Assyria — the brutal, pagan superpower — is *His tool*. He's sending it against "an hypocritical nation" (v. 6) to discipline His own people. But Assyria doesn't know it's a tool. Verse 7: "Yet he does not mean so." Assyria thinks it's conquering by its own genius.

So God poses one of the great rhetorical questions in Scripture, verse 15:

> "Shall the axe boast itself against him who chops with it?"

**Chuck Missler** loved this verse — he called it the ultimate corrective to every empire's press release. The axe doesn't swing itself. Nations, parliaments, courts, and superpowers are *instruments* in a hand they cannot see, and the moment the instrument starts boasting, God sets a termination date. Verses 16-19: when God is finished using Assyria, He burns it like a forest fire — so few trees left "a child may write them."

This is the comfort hiding inside the chapter: the same sovereignty that allows unjust lawmakers their season also **sets the limit of that season**. No decree of *aven* outlives its allotted time. Pharaoh learned it. Assyria learned it. Babylon learned it. Rome learned it.

---

## 🐍 The Unseen Layer

Pull the curtain back. Laws that prey on the defenseless aren't merely bad policy — they're the signature of the one Jesus called "a murderer from the beginning" (John 8:44). Ephesians 6:12 says our wrestling isn't against flesh and blood but against **rulers and authorities in heavenly places** — and it's striking that Paul uses *governmental* vocabulary for the demonic realm. The enemy has always sought to capture the engraving pen, because one wicked law does more damage than a thousand individual sins — it *disciples a population* into calling evil good (Isaiah 5:20, the woe right before this sequence began).

Which means the believer's primary weapon isn't outrage. It's the throne room. "First of all... prayers, intercessions... for kings and all who are in authority" (1 Timothy 2:1-2).

---

## 🌿 The Remnant Returns (vv. 20-27)

Right in the middle of judgment, verse 21: **"The remnant will return, the remnant of Jacob, to the Mighty God."**

In Hebrew that's **שְׁאָר יָשׁוּב (She'ar-Yashuv)** — which is the *name of Isaiah's own son* (7:3). Isaiah had been walking around Jerusalem for years with a living sermon holding his hand. God never loses His faithful core. And notice where the remnant returns *to*: **El Gibbor**, "Mighty God" — the exact title given to the Child of Isaiah 9:6 just a few verses earlier. The remnant doesn't return to national greatness. It returns to a *Person*.

And verse 27 gives the promise generations of believers have clung to: "the yoke will be destroyed because of the anointing" — the oil, the anointed one. Oppression has an expiration date, and it's stamped with Messiah's name.

---

## 🥾 The March That Stops (vv. 28-34)

The chapter closes with a cinematic sequence — **Dr. Bill Creasy** walks this one like a tour guide, because it's pure geography: Aiath, Migron, Michmash, Geba... Isaiah names the towns falling one by one as Assyria's army marches the ridge route south toward Jerusalem, panic spreading village by village, until verse 32 — the invader stands at Nob, close enough to *shake his fist at Mount Zion*.

And then verse 33: "Behold, the Lord... will lop off the bough with terror."

History records it: 185,000 Assyrian soldiers dead in one night outside Jerusalem's walls (Isaiah 37:36). The axe got within sight of the city of God — and the Lumberjack took it back.

---

## 🕊️ Closing Reflection

Isaiah 10 is for believers living in a nation where injustice carries a legal seal and a celebration party. It tells us three things: God *sees* every engraved decree of *aven* and has already asked its authors, "What will you do in the day of visitation?" God *governs* even the powers that don't know His name — no axe boasts forever. And God *keeps* a remnant whose hope was never the legislature anyway, but El Gibbor, the Mighty God who stops armies at the city limits.

So we grieve what's celebrated, we intercede for those holding the pen, we defend the voiceless with our own hands and voices — and we refuse despair, because the yoke is already scheduled for destruction "because of the anointing."

---

And speaking of the anointing — what comes immediately after the forest of Assyria is chopped down? Isaiah 11:1: *"There shall come forth a Rod from the stem of Jesse, and a Branch shall grow out of his roots."* Every other tree falls... and one shoot grows. Next chapter is the Messiah Himself — the sevenfold Spirit, the wolf and the lamb, the earth full of the knowledge of the LORD. The best chapter in the neighborhood is up next. 🌱
