## AI Response

Great question -- this is a rich thread in Second Temple and early Christian tradition.

You're right that the **Life of Adam and Eve** (Latin: *Vita Adae et Evae*) has exactly this scene. Satan himself narrates it: God created Adam in His image, and Michael brought Adam before the angels and said "Worship the image of the Lord God." Michael bowed first, then called Satan to do the same. Satan refused, saying "I will not worship one inferior and younger than me -- I existed before him; he ought to worship *me*." That refusal becomes the origin of his fall and his hatred of humanity.

You're also right that it's not canon. But the tradition is surprisingly widespread:

- **The Quran** (Surah 2:34, 7:11-12, 15:28-33) tells essentially the same story -- God commands the angels to prostrate before Adam, Iblis refuses out of pride ("I am made of fire, he is made of clay").
- **2 Enoch 31:3-6** (also extracanonical) connects Satan's fall to envy of Adam's position.
- Several **Church Fathers** (Irenaeus, Tertullian, Augustine) discuss angelic jealousy toward humanity as a factor in the fall, though they ground it more in pride generally than in a specific "bow down" command.

What **is** canonical and interesting:

- **Hebrews 1:6** -- "Let all God's angels worship Him" (referring to the Son). Then **Hebrews 2:5-8** says the world to come is subjected not to angels but to *man*, and quotes Psalm 8 -- "You made him a little lower than the angels... you put everything under his feet." There's a real tension the author is working with: angels serve, but humanity inherits.
- **Ezekiel 28 / Isaiah 14** -- the traditional Satan's-fall passages root it in pride and desire to ascend above God, not specifically jealousy of man. But pride over *what*? The text doesn't spell it out.
- **Genesis 1:26-27** -- the *imago Dei* is given to humanity alone. No angel is ever described as bearing God's image. That's a staggering distinction.

So the canonical picture gives you the ingredients -- angels as servants, man as image-bearer and heir, Satan falling through pride -- but the extracanonical sources are the ones that connect the dots into the specific narrative of "God told the angels to honor Adam and Satan refused."

It's one of those cases where the tradition may be capturing a genuine theological logic even if it's not in the 66 books. The jealousy of the image-bearer fits everything we see in Genesis 3 -- Satan's first move is to attack the *image* relationship, tempting humans to distrust the God whose image they carry.
